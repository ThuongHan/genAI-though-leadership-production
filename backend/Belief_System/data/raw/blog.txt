=== Community Spotlight: Lennard Kooy ===


--- Building Lleverage, AI automation, and the search for product-market fit ---

Every month, we spotlight a member of our community. At KickstartAI, we believe that meaningful progress starts with individuals — engineers, researchers, founders, and practitioners — who are applying AI in bold, practical ways. Through our Community Spotlight series, we celebrate the people shaping the future of AI in the Netherlands: their work, their challenges, and the lessons they’re learning along the way.

This month, we’re featuring Lennard Kooy, co-founder and CEO of Lleverage — a startup making AI-powered automation accessible to everyone. From building with natural language to navigating the search for product-market fit, this is his story.

It’s been less than a year since Lennard Kooy co-founded Lleverage — an end-to-end AI platform that automates complex business processes without the need for developers. A serial founder, Lennard has built four SaaS startups to date, but Lleverage is the first one that has a 100% AI DNA.

“In all honesty, I think that if you're starting a SaaS company now and it’s not an AI company, there has to be a very good reason for it,” Lennard says.

He believes that, in five years time, 80% to 90% of what developers currently write in code will be created using natural language — making AI a fundamental technology for future SaaS startups.


=== The birth of Lleverage ===

The idea behind Lleverage came from firsthand experience. At his previous SaaS company in marketing technology, Storyteq, Lennard was already experimenting with generative AI. It felt like a natural fit: GenAI excels at language and visuals — the backbone of ads.

But when it came to actually integrating this technology into the product, Lennard and Badr Eddial (at the time, Storyteq’s co-founder and CTO) ran into a problem. Their team of developers struggled with the non-deterministic nature of AI and new concepts like RAG pipelines and vector databases.

“When we left Storyteq, we thought this was quite an interesting problem,” Lennard says. “So we started out with making a platform to bridge the gap between traditional developer skills and the power of AI.”

While Llevarge was born to simplify AI workloads for developers, the focus quickly expanded to a business audience, such as product owners and business experts.

The pivot followed early conversations with customers. Lennard and his team realized that, by automating business processes using just natural language, Leverage could directly cater to non-technical teams in need of technical solutions. The traction in their deal pipeline was significantly quicker with COOs and CEOs compared to CTOs, supporting this change in direction.


=== Under the hood ===

The first thing that Lleverage built was a workflow engine to facilitate data flow in automations. The team decided from the beginning to differentiate the company from chat interfaces like ChatGPT or Gemini and focus on the data processing pipelines rather than the end-user interface.

Lennard explains that the startup’s core technology is the workflow engine — primarily built with JavaScript and powered by Natural Language Processing (NLP).  Underneath this engine, there are multiple building blocks, from various AI models to vector databases like Weviate.

In essence, Lleverage offers “describe-and-build” AI workflow solutions. This is what it calls “vibe automation.”

Such an architecture doesn’t come without its challenges, according to Lennard. The complexity and volume of data processing in user-defined pipelines create significant technical demands. Behind the scenes, Lleverage has to ensure data integrity, handle retries for external API failures, and manage the non-deterministic nature of AI outputs — all while keeping the experience smooth for the user.

But the most complex problem Lleverage has faced so far isn’t a technical one.


=== The greatest early-stage challenge: finding product-market fit ===

“The biggest challenge for a company in our stage is finding the right product-market fit,” Lennard says. “It’s an iffy concept.”

“When do you feel that your product metrics are giving you the signals that your positioning is right for you? Or when do you think that there’s not enough real traction and you need to keep changing and do another iteration on the product or the business proposition?”

“It’s a constant struggle,” he adds.

Lennard also stresses that the search for product-market fit influences every part of the business: from the business model and the pricing strategy to the type of talent needed and the overall go-to-market motion.

“When transitioning to a sales-led enterprise play, for example, you need to think of a different organizational structure, with a focus on customer success, solutions engineers, and dedicated account executives,” he says.

He compares this to a product-led growth strategy, where the focus shifts towards growth marketing and community management.

Lleverage itself is now in-between the sales-led and the product-led growth motion.

“We’re consciously not making a decision right now,” Lennard explains. “But we’re going to make one in the following months. We can’t maintain this position for a long time, because it defocuses a small company like us.”

Besides market fit, Lennard mentions another challenge for early-stage companies: funding. The startup raised €3mn earlier this month, bringing its total investment to €5mn in less than a year. It has managed to avoid the pitfalls of the funding rollercoaster thanks to its seasoned founders who have already built and scaled successful companies.

But for first-time founders, attracting capital — especially in the AI space — is no easy task, Lennard says. “You have to convince VCs about the defensibility of your idea and that you can actually outgrow your competition.”


=== Building a startup is no bed of roses ===

When asked about the biggest lesson he’s learned on his journey with Lleverage, Lennard doesn’t hesitate: “I had forgotten how painful it is to build a startup.”

He cautions prospective founders against romanticizing the concept of a startup. “Make sure you actually want it and talk to people who have done it before or are doing it now.”

At the same time, he advises against inaction. “If you really want it, just go for it, because sitting on the fence for eternity is also something you shouldn’t do.”

Another important lesson for Lennard is figuring out how to handle competition — especially in a space as crowded and fast-moving as AI.

“I don’t open my LinkedIn every day anymore, because every time I do, I see another competitor,” he jokes. He explains that being confronted with competition every day is not only draining, but also distracting.

“You have to find the right balance between listening to those noises and just believe in what you’re building. There’s a lot of value in perseverance.”


=== Growing the Dutch AI ecosystem needs more work ===

Lennard contrasts the Netherlands with France, where active government support, investor focus, and ambitious infrastructure — like the soon-to-launch world’s largest startup campus — are transforming the country into Europe’s AI powerhouse.

“I feel in the last six months we’re making steps, but before that, there wasn’t a lot of noise around the ecosystem,” he says. “I think we can still do more.”

Lennard says that initiatives like KickstartAI have a role to play. He joined the community when he attended one of our events earlier this year. “I think that communities can be a big boost to the ecosystem and they’re a great way for people to feel that they are part of something.”

=== Community Spotlight: Michael Dubbeldam ===


--- Building FlowCube: Inside vision AI for traffic management ---

Every month, we spotlight a member of our community . At KickstartAI, we believe that meaningful progress starts with individuals — engineers, researchers, founders, and practitioners — who are applying AI in bold, practical ways. Through our Community Spotlight series, we celebrate the people shaping the future of AI in the Netherlands: their work, their challenges, and the lessons they’re learning along the way.

This month, we’re featuring Michael Dubbeldam, Domain Architect at Technolution — the company behind FlowCube, a camera-based traffic sensor with embedded deep learning models. From solving real-world mobility challenges in Copenhagen to pioneering vision AI, this is his story.

Michael Dubbeldam started out as a software engineer and, later on, moved into software architecture. He joined Technolution in 2005, where he’s now leading the AI competence group. His interest in AI was first sparked by a traffic management project for the city of Copenhagen in 2017.

“The city wanted a system that could perform as well for cyclists as it did for cars,” Michael recalls. “They needed to evaluate how well the streets were serving cyclists and collect information on how much time cyclists need to go from one side of the city to the other via a particular route.”

At that time, the only way to gather that data was by physically recording videos and then manually analyzing the footage to track individual cyclists.

Naturally, that process took an enormous amount of time. “At that point, we thought: if only there was a system that could do something like that. If only we could use a camera to see what we see.”

Michael explains the rationale behind that idea.

“Traditional traffic management systems relying on traffic light input provide limited information,” he says. “But as humans, going into the field and seeing what’s happening, we can perceive so much more.”

A camera could potentially replace human eyes, but there was a challenge: enabling a computer to provide and translate all the necessary information.

Michael believed that vision AI could be the answer.

“I thought that with AI, computers can see the same things or understand the same things as humans. So you don’t need to adjust the world to computers; instead, computers can adjust to the human world.”

“That’s why I really got into AI. I wanted to understand how it works, build my own models, and push the boundaries of what’s possible.”

The Copenhagen challenge marked the first vision AI project for Michael and the Technolution team. It also served as the inspiration for a new product journey.


=== FlowCube: from concept to product ===

Michael’s first foray into vision AI gave birth to the concept of  FlowCube —  a camera-based traffic sensor with embedded deep learning models that measure traffic flows of vehicles, bicycles, and pedestrians.

“When we first started, it wasn’t a cube,” he says. Initially, the team created a device with a camera that processed images locally, converting them into data. The system would send information in the form of “I saw a cyclist with a blue coat at this location at this moment,” Michael explains. That way, it could analyze traffic without storing or transmitting any raw images.

“Over time, we designed a nice cube and kept making the system smarter and more efficient,” he says.

It took about two years to reach the minimum viable product stage. “Once you’ve had that, you think that you’re done and can take on the world,” Michael adds. “But as you build, you learn more about what you need to do. We’ve kept refining and improving the system ever since, and I expect it will be even better five years from now.”

Today, FlowCube comes as a box measuring 13x13x13cm. Inside, there’s an inexpensive commodity camera, a computer, an Nvidia processor, and a 4G mobile connection.


=== Building with Siamese models ===

FlowCube captures images and is able to detect objects and people and map their movement in real time.

“We get information like: there’s a cyclist with a blue coat and a bag on the bike at this location and time,” Michael says. The device then converts this type of data into what the team calls a “match code” — a statistical representation of an object’s appearance.

These match codes are sent to a cloud server, where data from multiple FlowCube devices is combined to reconstruct city-wide mobility patterns. This way, the system provides a complete picture of traffic across all road users, while preserving the privacy of individuals.

“We don’t track people,” Michael emphasizes. “It’s about statistical patterns and comparing likelihoods.” For example, that a cyclist at point AI is statistically similar to another one at point B.

To achieve this, the team uses Siamese models — trained not to classify objects, but to assess how alike two inputs are. In this case, not whether two people are both cyclists, but whether they might be the same individual seen in different locations.

That’s useful for what’s called “person re-identification,” where the goal is to determine how much two people resemble each other.

The system is trained using labeled images, so it learns what features actually help distinguish individuals. “For example, it might learn that background or the fact that someone is on two wheels isn’t helpful, but clothing color or a backpack might be.”

Michael’s team is also working to make the system’s decisions more interpretable. “We’re now exploring how we can name those distinguishing features explicitly — like ‘white sneakers’ or ‘blue coat’ — so the reasoning is understandable not just to machines, but to people too.”

For model training, Technolution creates its own datasets. “For the re-identification models, for example, we have a dataset of approximately 250,000 images and 15,000 identities,” Michael says. “We’ve also needed to adapt our training data to the local context.” For instance, vehicles in the US, include pick-up trucks and yellow school buses — which are uncommon in Europe. In Nordic countries, cyclists typically wear helmets unlike in the Netherlands.


=== Challenges ===

Michael identifies three main challenges in building and developing FlowCube. The first one is hardware: building a device that’s robust, scalable, easy to manufacture, and consistently reliable in the field.

Another difficulty is creating models that generalize well across different environments. Something that works in one place may not work elsewhere without retraining.

Finally, the third major challenge lies in the development of the Siamese models. “Most research on the technology of person or object re-identification works with closed-world benchmarks, where a match is guaranteed. You simply have to find the best one.”

But in real open-world environments, this isn’t the case. “You don’t know if a match exists,” Michael explains.

This gap between closed- versus open-world applications meant that the team couldn’t rely on existing models or benchmarks. “We had to define our models, layers, cost functions, and training to get them to do what we needed them to do.”


=== AI’s role in multimodal traffic management ===

For Michael, the biggest benefit AI brings to traffic management is access to human-level information. Traditional sensors can’t distinguish a bus from a car based on visual cues. “Vision AI can,” he emphasizes.

AI-based sensors can make smarter decisions and optimize the overall traffic management system — for example, by adjusting traffic lights to prioritize buses. This optimization is particularly important for multimodal countries, with a large number of cyclists and pedestrians, like the Netherlands.

Equally important is to comply with regulations like the GDPR in order to increase both customer and public trust.

“Images never leave the device — they are processed on the edge and then discarded. We don’t store identifiable information,” Michael says.

“But we also need to involve the cities or municipalities we’re working with in our processes, so they understand our privacy safeguards.”

Michael joined the KickstartAI community only recently, but already sees value.

“There aren’t many people who are deeply in this field and who are bringing lab work to reality. So it’s good to see how others manage the same things we struggle with. To me, that’s really educational. And I’m reading a lot that I find very interesting.”

=== Shield or sword? The role of AI in safety and security ===

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

Today, AI plays a critical role in strengthening our safety across diverse domains, from cyber defense to combating financial crime and protecting national security. Yet this same capability also exposes us to serious risks: AI can serve as both a powerful shield and a vicious sword, depending on who wields it.

This isn’t just about speculative, existential dangers. It’s about tangible, immediate threats stemming from misuse: generative AI tools enabling deepfakes, AI-driven social engineering scams, or autonomous systems deployed with unclear oversight.

This edition explores how AI is being used to reinforce security in cybersecurity, financial fraud detection, threat prediction, and military defense operations. It also looks inward at the field of AI safety: how experts are working to make these systems more robust, explainable, and aligned with human intent.

To better understand the current landscape in the Netherlands, we spoke with four leading experts working at the intersection of AI, safety, and security:

Stjepan Picek , Associate Professor of Digital Security at Radboud University

Stjepan Picek , Associate Professor of Digital Security at Radboud University

Iskander Hannivoort , Founder of Manukai , Investor, and Airline Captain at Transavia

Natasha Govender-Ropert , Head of AI – Financial Economic Crime at Rabobank

Natasha Govender-Ropert , Head of AI – Financial Economic Crime at Rabobank

Captain van den Broek of the Netherlands Royal Army , and PhD researcher at the University of Twente

--- AI in cybersecurity: “a cat and mouse game” ---

AI is already integral to cybersecurity, from simpler tasks such as filtering spam to anomaly detection and defense automation. But the same technology is also being used to craft more personalized, scalable attacks — especially following the advent of Generative AI.

“We’re seeing a large amount of scam attempts using deepfakes for social engineering purposes,” says Stjepan Picek, Associate Professor of Digital Security at Radboud University. Attackers are also using GenAI tools to automate malware generation, enabling “more diverse attacks with less effort and on a massive scale.”

As to how cybersecurity systems can keep up, Picek says “it’s always been a cat and mouse game.”

“The main difference now is that the cycles are faster,” he adds. A new attack emerges, a defense follows, but another attack soon breaks through.

“With this incremental approach, it’s difficult to say that anything will change fundamentally,” Picek notes. Even if a perfectly secure system existed at the moment, it could still be vulnerable to future threats. “We, as defenders, constantly need to monitor, test, and improve.”

Security of AI and AI for security

Picek and his team at the university are working on two research areas: the security of AI and AI for security. “In the first case, we assess various kinds of machine learning algorithms, try to attack them, and by doing that, we try to propose new defenses that would strengthen them against attacks.”

In the AI for security field, they’re using machine learning and deep learning to perform different types of attacks (such as side-channel attacks), in order to test the security and resilience of various devices like smart or credit cards.

Accuracy trumps safety

One challenge in the cybersecurity space, according to Picek, is that safety by design in AI systems is quite difficult to achieve.

The first reason why is simple. “Machine learning was never designed a priori to be safe and secure. It was designed to be accurate,” he says. The second reason is that safety by design always involves a tradeoff. “Your algorithm will potentially become less accurate. You will spend more time and energy resources to train it. You will need more human expertise to make it.”

As widespread attacks are still relatively limited, he notes, many companies aren’t sufficiently concerned about potential risks. They’re often unwilling to invest in making their systems more secure, especially when this might reduce algorithmic performance.

As AI adoption surges, he warns that security is often an afterthought. “Everyone wants to deploy AI — but safety and security still aren’t high on the list of priorities.”


--- Smarter threat prediction ---

As both digital and physical security challenges grow more complex and intertwined, traditional tools are no longer enough. AI is shifting the paradigm — from reactive monitoring to proactive, real-time threat prediction.

Startups like Manukai are helping drive this shift. The company’s AI-based threat prediction tool — set to go live later this year — aims to alert business travelers about risks ranging from civil unrest to terrorism, before they occur.

“There are certain unique challenges in security at the moment that AI can help address,” Iskander Hannivoort, founder of Manukai, says. He explains that AI enables 24/7 monitoring, faster pattern recognition, and predictive insights — all critical in areas with geopolitical and operational security risks for businesses.

Real-time risk prediction

Manukai’s proprietary system, based on an adapted open-source model, pulls data from sources like news outlets and social media. It analyzes patterns to forecast where and when a threat may occur, using a time- and location-based logic. The goal is full automation, providing users with direct advice — from postponing a trip to navigating to a safe area — without human analysts in the loop.

To be effective, however, the system must be unbiased, Hannivoort explains, though he acknowledges that bias is hard to define. “We need to assess every bit of information before it goes into our prediction model. Is it true and verifiable? What’s the underlying narrative we can detect?”

A second layer of Manukai’s tool is personalization. “Your risk profile is dependent on who you are,” he says. “Our system will know which field you are in and for which groups or countries you or your company’s intellectual property is a high-value target.”

Challenges

A major challenge, Hannivort admits, is understanding how the AI arrives at its predictions, functioning like a black box. “You can only verify the conclusions and try to relatively make sure that they’re free of errors and hallucinations.”

There’s also the question of responsibility. Predictive tools risk both false alarms and missed threats — and with that comes liability. That’s why Manukai positions its system as a decision-support tool, not a replacement for human judgment.

Looking ahead, Hannivoort sees AI-based threat prediction playing a key role in long-term strategic planning — helping companies, governments, and societies identify faster existing and emerging threats.


--- Combating financial crime ---

In the complex realm of financial crime, AI acts as a double-edged sword. Natasha Govender-Ropert from Rabobank, shares how the team leverages AI and analytics to combat threats while navigating critical ethical considerations.

“Banks are highly regulated, which means that there is a significant number of rules and laws we must adhere to, next to having oversight agencies,” she says. “This also influences the type of AI algorithms we implement. We don’t use cutting-edge technology, unless we’re able to explain it.”

AI for anomaly detection

Fraud detection has traditionally relied on rule-based systems, which typically flag suspicious transactions based on certain rules and thresholds. For example, transfers over a certain amount of money over a short period of time.

“The issue with rule-based systems is that they give rise to a large number of false positives, which analysts have to manually review,” she adds. “So our biggest focus area is reducing the amount of false positives.”

For this reason, the fraud team is investigating the use of anomaly detection and real-time analytics for fraud detection. “We’re building traditional AI systems in combination with rule-based systems to create a more robust detection algorithm, so we can better understand if our customers’ behavior deviates from normal patterns,” she says.

Compared to rule-based systems with fixed logic, machine learning models operate in multi-dimensional feature spaces, enabling them to learn complex patterns and define more adaptive decision boundaries for fraud detection.

Generative AI: benefits and risks

Govender-Ropert sees GenAI as a valuable tool to improve day-to-day operations. It can significantly reduce manual, tedious work for fraud analysts by assisting with investigations, summarizations, and report writing. This allows human experts to focus on more complex, high-value tasks.

"For instance, we have a chatbot that guides analysts during investigations and retrieves relevant information from multiple databases, providing a clear and concise overview that saves time.”

But the advent of Generative AI has also introduced unprecedented challenges in combating financial crime, Govender-Ropert emphasizes. Fraudsters can now use GenAI to create hyper-realistic fake documents, videos, and even voices. Scams have also become more convincing, with phishing emails and messages that mimic real corporate communications almost perfectly.

Detection of deepfakes remains a developing area. Even experienced professionals may overlook subtle indicators, particularly when not actively assessing content for authenticity.


--- AI in defense: autonomy with human oversight ---

AI is emerging as the next frontier in modern warfare and defence. Earlier this year, State Secretary for Defence Gijs Tuinman announced his intention to invest €310mn in unmanned systems and innovative technologies such as AI.

AI can have several applications within the Dutch armed forces, according to Captain van den Broek of the Royal Netherlands Army. These applications include image recognition, terrain analysis, as well as planning and decision support for units.

Experimenting with autonomous robots

Captain van den Broek also leads the Dutch army’s development of military autonomy and AI. As part of his work in the army as well as his PhD at the University of Twente, he’s currently experimenting with autonomous robots — such as unmanned, remotely controlled vehicles. Such robots can be used for tasks including reconnaissance missions and cleaning high-danger zones like landmines and radioactive areas.

Controlled autonomy

The level of autonomy is entirely use-case dependent, Captain van den Broek says. “Is it just navigation or are we talking about swarming missions with multiple vehicles, or even drones all working together?”

He emphasizes that robots and AI can assist with data and a variety of tasks, but the use of lethal force must always remain a human decision. This principle guides the development of autonomous systems in the Dutch armed forces, where there is no place for self-learning weapons that decide in real-time who or what to target.

All model development and training must be completed and verified in advance. “During execution, you need a fully trained and validated model that has passed all necessary checks,” Captain van den Broek explains. This ensures what he calls "controlled autonomy:" systems that are capable but predictable — and always under human command.

Trust through transparency

Trust is central. “If we want to use autonomous robots,” he says, “commanders must trust that they will behave as expected.” Part of Captain van den Broek’s work involves not only refining the technology, but also showing unit commanders exactly how these systems work and what decisions they’re making in practice.

“These systems should support, not replace human decision-making,” he emphasizes.


--- What does “robust” AI really mean? ---

According to the experts we spoke with, the essence of AI robustness depends on the field and the use case for which a model is deployed.

From a cybersecurity perspective, model robustness ideally means resilience against attacks, Picek says. “Once you train your model in the presence of a specific attack, you make it more robust against similar attacks that might happen,” he explains. Achieving this kind of robustness depends not only on the model architecture and threat type, but also on who controls the training process — whether it’s handled internally or outsourced.

“In military language, AI or autonomy robustness means that the systems will always work across different environments, scenarios, and threats,” Captain van den Broek says. This includes high performance in various terrains, weather conditions, and different types of tasks such as traditional warfare or peacekeeping missions.

“Robustness starts with data quality and relevance," says Govender-Ropert. It’s also about checking a model’s thresholds, verifying its behavior through testing, and actively monitoring it after deployment — especially since models can drift over time as new data enters the system.


--- Building ethical and responsible AI ---

AI systems can reinforce bias, invade privacy, or make unsafe decisions,  unless ethics are embedded from day one.

Responsible AI in practice

For Govender-Ropert, ethical AI refers to the broader societal and philosophical question of what is good or harmful, while responsible AI is about putting those values into practice. At Rabobank, this means building systems that are transparent, explainable, secure, and fair. These principles are embedded into the bank’s model development lifecycle.

But fairness isn't a universal standard and different organizations may interpret it differently, Govender-Ropert emphasizes. What matters is that teams make conscious decisions about what fairness means in their context and translate them into clear guidelines for development and testing.

“We need to understand that the decisions we make, how we build these models, and how we use them can impact people’s lives.”

Ethics start with intent, not compliance

Stjepan Picek believes that ethical and safe AI begins with intent, not just compliance with regulations such as the EU’s AI Act. “We, as humans, developers, owners, and users of models, need to acknowledge that AI security is an important goal,” he says.

Fairness is a critical element for Picek as well. “At the moment, we see more negative consequences from biased algorithms than from insecure algorithms.”

Guardrails without stifling innovation

Iskander Hannivoort agrees that safety and fairness are essential — but warns that over-regulation may hamper Europe’s competitiveness in the AI race. “We want to innovate, but we also need guardrails,” he says. “That balance is hard.”

Keeping humans in the loop

In the defense sector, Captain van den Broek stresses that responsible AI means incorporating human oversight at every level: from commanders who must trust the systems they deploy, to political and strategic leaders who decide when and how these technologies are used, and ultimately to the society they serve.

“Transparency, explainability, and human oversight: that’s what responsible autonomy looks like,” he says.

Ethical deployment, he argues, is about protecting people. Yet in our imperfect world, these same technologies can also be weaponized in violation of human rights. “This is a serious ethical debate,” Captain van den Broek says. “But not developing these technologies could leave us at a disadvantage against enemies who do.”


--- The Netherlands: positioned to lead or lagging behind? ---

The Netherlands has made visible progress in embedding ethical governance into AI development, especially in the banking sector, says Govender-Ropert, speaking from her personal experience in ING and Rabobank.

But challenges remain. She points to a lack of public trust and the need for AI education.

Trust isn’t the only challenge. Captain van den Broek sees ambition in the defense sector — with rising investment, collaborations, and experimentation — but acknowledges that the Netherlands is still in a catch-up phase. “Swarming autonomous tanks don’t exist yet,” he notes. “We know we need to make major steps in this field.”

Iskander Hannivoort is more blunt: “There’s a huge backlog.” He says Dutch organizations still underestimate security — not just in AI, but across the board. “The Netherlands is a safe country, and people aren’t used to violence or danger.”

Cybersecurity researcher Stjepan Picek adds that while the Netherlands is not lagging behind its neighboring countries, it isn’t leading either. “We’re in the middle. === Network analysis: mapping the Netherlands' threat landscape ===

By Cascha van Wanrooij

Historically, the Netherlands became world-renowned for mastering water management after the 1953 floods. But today’s challenges are not only greater, but also more complex and intertwined: cyber attacks, ecological threats, and societal polarization. DenkWerk , a Dutch think tank, recently published " Resilience by Design ” — a comprehensive analysis of emerging threats the Netherlands must prepare for.

DenkWerk’s approach involved building a network that visualizes how these threats interconnect and that identifies which ones can cause a ripple effect. The methodology shares some similarities with the World Economic Forum's annual risk report , but with three key differences. Firstly, it focuses specifically on the Netherlands. Secondly, it relies on existing data from ministries and research bureaus rather than expert surveys. And thirdly, it maps the directional relationships between threats.

Unlike traditional risk models which plot threats on two axes (impact and probability) to identify priorities, DenkWerk's directional analysis adds interconnectedness as a crucial third dimension. This way, it reveals how threats cascade through the system, illuminating upstream intervention points that the standard two-dimensional view misses.


>>> The technical challenge <<<

My contribution to DenkWerk's report was building this network. Specifically, I developed a node analysis tool that would help identify the central nodes ("knooppunten"): the threats that are likely to trigger other important threats. By identifying these central nodes, we could focus preventive efforts where they would have the greatest impact.

I split the project into three phases:

Programmatically scrape and download report PDFs from government and research bureau websites

Programmatically scrape and download report PDFs from government and research bureau websites

Process content through LLMs to identify threats and their relationships

Process content through LLMs to identify threats and their relationships

Construct the network and perform centrality analysis

Construct the network and perform centrality analysis

Let's dig into phase two, where things got properly interesting.


>>> Initial approach and cost constraints <<<

After collecting approximately 100,000 PDFs, I immediately hit the universal project constraint: cost. My first instinct was frame-semantic parsing ( here's an elegant Science Advances paper demonstrating this ), which doesn't require massive decoder-only LLMs. But Dutch fine-tuned language models are scarce, so GPT-style models became the practical choice.

Starting with GPT-4o mini for cost efficiency , I designed a pipeline where the model processed document chunks to produce structured outputs: dictionaries containing cause-effect pairs with verbatim citations. N-gram matching verified citations against source text. Topic modeling then aggregated these relationships into broader threat categories, between which we can track relationships.


>>> The statistical challenge of rare events <<<

The pipeline functioned well for most cases, but revealed a fundamental issue: causal relationships between threats are rare in these documents. This scarcity creates a difficult statistical problem. Even with 99% precision/recall, processing thousands of chunks means accumulating substantial errors. With rare events, that 1% error rate becomes highly problematic. The model's mistakes weren't obvious hallucinations but subtle misinterpretations. One telling example: documents noted decreased CO2 emissions during COVID-19, particularly in aviation. The model cited this as evidence that pandemics worsen climate change, completely inverting the actual relationship.

Multiple mitigation strategies failed to resolve these issues:

Extensive prompt refinement

Extensive prompt refinement

Alternative chunking strategies

Alternative chunking strategies

Decomposing tasks into sequential LLM calls

Decomposing tasks into sequential LLM calls

Building comprehensive testing frameworks

Building comprehensive testing frameworks

Each fix introduced new problems. My prompts evolved into lengthy lists of prohibitions rather than clear instructions. The core issue remained: when searching for rare signals, even small error rates compound into significant noise.


>>> The bigger model solution <<<

Then Gemini 2.5 Flash (preview) arrived. At $15 cents per million tokens versus $10 cents for GPT-4o mini, it wasn't dramatically more expensive. But the performance difference was striking.

I scrapped my elaborate prompt engineering and started fresh with simple, clear instructions. It just worked. This is Rich Sutton's " bitter lesson " in action: clever engineering usually loses to approaches that better leverage computational resources. All my GPT-4o mini optimizations were essentially band-aids on a model that wasn't quite powerful enough for the task.

Lesson learned: start with the best model there is on a data subset. Establish what's possible, then optimize for cost if needed. Don't start cheap and try to engineer your way up, you'll waste time and sanity. Donald Knuth’s software engineering lessons can apply to machine learning as well.


>>> What the network revealed <<<

Among other things, the final analysis uncovered that cyber attacks, hybrid warfare, and societal polarization form a self-reinforcing triangle at the center of our threat landscape. This mirrors the recent Dutch security agency reports . It’s an  unsurprising result given that these sorts of reports also belong in our corpus, but it is a nice, if somewhat unsettling, validation check.


>>> Building the interactive visualization <<<

The network data required an interface that would make exploration intuitive while maintaining transparency about sources. I used Claude Sonnet and Gemini 2.5 Pro to help quickly develop a static website with interactive graph visualization.

The primary visualization uses Reagraph , a WebGL-based graph library. While generally excellent, it had limitations in our case. For example, the clickable area for edges was too small. Nevertheless, modern LLMs proved very useful here, helping me quickly understand the library's architecture and create a forked version with improved interaction targets. This approach — using LLMs to navigate and modify unfamiliar codebases, significantly sped up development.

When SVG export functionality became a requirement, I took a pragmatic approach. Since Reagraph's WebGL foundation made direct SVG export challenging, I had the LLMs build a simple SVG-based graph renderer that matched our visual design. Once again, this shows the use of LLMs for these kinds of “standalone” projects.

Most importantly, every connection in the graph links back to its source. Click any threat relationship and you'll see the exact report and passage that identified it. Clicking any threat relationship reveals the original government report and specific citation. This design ensures the graph provides a transparent look into human-authored analysis rather than representing an opaque AI interpretation.

The complete dataset and interactive visualizations are available at: https://denkwerk-kickstartai.nl/knooppuntenanalyse/


>>> Concluding thoughts <<<

This project was genuinely fun to work on: a problem where AI served to connect human insights rather than generate synthetic ones. With LLMs handling the work of extraction and relationship mapping, allowing us to scale to thousands of documents.

The experience confirmed that core software engineering principles are just as relevant in ML work. Start with the best available tool, establish what's possible, then optimize.

Finally, building interactive websites with AI assistance has become really enjoyable. The usual cycle of coding, testing, and iterating speeds up when you can quickly navigate unfamiliar libraries and prototype ideas. Static sites with rich interactivity hit a sweet spot for LLM-assisted development. Instead of spending hours reading documentation, you can immediately test ideas and explore alternatives — removing the hurdles that typically slow down experimentation.

=== Community Spotlight: Alexander Maas and Marinus Israël ===


--- Bridging AI’s business gap ---

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

Every month, we spotlight a member of our community . At KickstartAI, we believe that meaningful progress starts with individuals — engineers, researchers, founders, and practitioners — who are applying AI in bold, practical ways. Through our Community Spotlight series, we celebrate the people shaping the future of AI in the Netherlands: their work, their challenges, and the lessons they’re learning along the way.

This month, we’re featuring Marinus Israël and Alexander Maas, co-founders of Algemi — a generative AI agency helping businesses bridge the gap between AI’s potential and its practical use. From their first collaboration on a refugee support platform to launching a company focused on practical AI, this is their story.

Alexander Maas and Marinus Israël met in 2022 while working together on the development of RefugeeHelp — an online platform which helps refugees find their way to the Netherlands.

Following a fruitful collaboration during that project (and an even more fruitful coffee meetup), the duo decided to build a company together. In 2024, they founded Algemi, a generative AI agency aiming to help businesses adopt AI solutions and harness the technology’s full potential.

“We saw a gap in what AI can do and what businesses are using it for. So we thought ‘well, let's start a company together to try and fill that gap,’” Alexander says.


--- The journey towards Algemi ---

Alexander’s passion for AI began early, but he chose to study aerospace engineering and spent several years at Airbus Defense and Space, contributing to satellite missions. He continued exploring software and AI as side projects, launching several startups alongside his aerospace work.

Eventually, this led him to shift careers and join a software consulting firm, where he worked as a senior software engineer and, later on, as tech lead and CTO.

Marinus’ interest in digital technology also started early on in his life. He built his first website when he was around 12 years old, and created his first company — in web hosting — when he was 16. He has since founded multiple startups and is specialized in digital strategy and product management and development.

“What we’ve both seen in our past,” Alexander says, “is that 90% of initiatives in software and digital product development have failed. The reason why has mostly to do with the fact that companies don’t think about the end customer.”

“And we see that a lot of companies currently trying to use AI are making the same kind of mistake. They focus too much on small efficiency gains. They forget the huge impact it will have on how work is performed and what is considered valuable by customers in the future.”

This is what the duo has set out to do differently with Algemi. The startup both helps companies identify AI use cases and builds custom AI and software solutions. Currently Algemi consists of a core team of four, supported by a vetted freelance pool.


--- Identifying AI use cases ---

According to Marinus, Algemi’s approach to identifying where AI can bring value relies on combining knowledge and effort.

“From our side, we know a lot about AI and what it can do. We also have a pretty broad understanding of how different types of businesses work,” he explains.

However, the most important factor, he says, is gaining a deep understanding of the business from the inside, which can only be done in close collaboration with Algemi’s clients. “They know their processes. They know their customers. They know the pain points and what they envision for the future.”

To guide their work, Marinus and Alexander use a framework that divides AI use cases in three parts. The first is efficiency gains — using AI to optimize existing operations. The second part is growth, looking at previously unfeasible products or services that AI can enable. The third part focuses on where AI itself may put a business at risk.

Once the team identifies the potential use cases, they pick the ones that can bring value in the short term. “We like to focus first on the low-hanging fruit,” Alexander says. “This provides customers with an easy first experience with AI. And with AI developing so quickly, what is now difficult to solve might be easy in the next few months.”

The team then builds a proof of concept to test whether their AI solution is feasible. The next phase is developing a Minimum Viable Product (MVP), involving the end users as much as possible throughout the process.

“We let them try it out, collaborate closely, and learn what tools they already use and how the solution can fit into their existing workflow,” Alexander explains. “A tool is used most often and effectively when it is easy to apply and fits well within your normal workflow.”

Following the MVP’s launch, the duo focuses on fine-tuning and improving based on user feedback.


--- Putting LLMs to work ---

When building AI solutions, Algemi leverages existing LLMs, selects the model that best fits the use case, and fine-tunes it accordingly. For most of the applications they’re building, the team uses OpenAI’s GPT models.

“LLMs are starting to get their own personalities,” Marinus says. “For instance, OpenAI’s GPT models are strong all-rounders, Claude is great for software engineering, and Gemini is especially effective with health-related tasks.”

In some cases, they already know by experience which model will work best, but they have also developed a test suite to match the right LLM and the right use case.

This test suite also helps evaluate use cases. “What we do is create a human-curated test set — a list of questions with expected answers,” Marinus explains. “Sometimes the answer is straightforward, like ‘yes,’ ‘no,’ or a number. Other times, it’s a bit more fuzzy.”

The team runs these questions through different models and compares their output to the expected answers. This allows them to see how well a model — or the overall use case — performs.

It also helps the duo fine-tune the models to improve accuracy and, importantly, gives them a way to show clients measurable results. “We can say, ‘This model gets 90% of the answers right, for example, and we can prove it,’” Marinus adds.

“In most cases, almost right is still wrong, however,” Alexander adds. For this reason, Algemi is conducting human or automated quality checks, aiming to get the correct result 100% of the time with minimum human effort.


--- Advice to AI founders ---

Building an AI company comes with unique challenges, one of the biggest being the speed at which the sector is moving. The premise is simple: what is an opportunity at the moment might no longer be an opportunity in just a few months from now.

A significant learning for Marinus and Alexander has been that sometimes it’s better to procrastinate building your own generally applicable tools. With these tools it is better to wait and see what kind of groundwork gets developed, and make use of that.

Marinus advises founders to start with a niche they understand well — whether it’s a specific industry or process — and combine that domain knowledge with AI’s capabilities. “I’d say, don’t go big on general AI because you’ll need a lot of money and you’re up against fierce competition from Silicon Valley.”

Alex shares a key insight (originally from Sam Altman) that he wishes he had known earlier: it's better to build products that benefit from more advanced AI, rather than compete with it. “For example if you build a product for a hospital, focus on integrating it into their systems using existing AI models over developing your own custom models. A more intelligent model is bound to come around in a few months which will only make your product more effective — instead of replacing it.”


--- The Dutch AI ecosystem ---

While the Netherlands is strong in leveraging existing AI models, it falls behind in foundational AI development, Alexander and Marinus note. They both advocate for further investment into building data centers in the country and advancing core AI capabilities.

“It’s something that’s going to be a strategic resource at some point,” Marinus says. “You don’t want half of your country to be relying on technology that’s hosted in a data center somewhere else. I also think it’d be a great idea to train a Dutch LLM.”

He nevertheless remains optimistic, seeing excitement across the sector and the creation of various AI communities, which could eventually lead to a single, all-encompassing one.

Marinus particularly appreciates the meetups of the KickstartAI community. “It’s a great opportunity to mingle, easily present what you’re doing, get to know what other people are working on, and keep up to date with developments.”

Alexander also sees value in exchanging ideas during the meetups, especially when it comes to creating awareness about the necessity of quickly implementing AI  within companies. “It’s interesting to see how other businesses are approaching this,” he says. “Sharing this knowledge is very valuable, and I think the market is large enough for all companies to survive. It’s more about winning together by solving the issue [of creating awareness for AI adoption] than about giving away our competing advantage.”

=== EU AI Act: rules, risks, and the innovation question ===

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

In 2024, the European Union adopted the world’s first comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence. Known as the EU AI Act, the landmark law has marked a pivotal moment in the global conversation around how advanced AI systems should be governed, by whom, and under what conditions.

At a time when AI capabilities are advancing rapidly, the goal of the AI Act, according to the European Commission, is to ensure the development of responsible and trustworthy AI, while supporting innovation. For developers and companies building AI systems in or for the EU market, the implications are wide-ranging — and in some cases, still unclear, raising questions about feasibility, unintended consequences, and the cost of compliance in a fast-moving field.

This article explores the architecture of the AI Act, its intended impact, the friction it creates in implementation, and the broader debate it has sparked, drawing on conversations with four experts across business, law, and policy:

Jeannette Gorzala , CEO and founder of AI ACT NOW ,  Vice Chair at the AI Board of the Austrian Government, and Member of the GPAI Working Groups of the EU AI Office

Jeannette Gorzala , CEO and founder of AI ACT NOW ,  Vice Chair at the AI Board of the Austrian Government, and Member of the GPAI Working Groups of the EU AI Office

Michel van Leeuwen , Director of AI at the Dutch Ministry of Justice and Security

Michel van Leeuwen , Director of AI at the Dutch Ministry of Justice and Security

Monika Tomczak-Górlikowska , Chief Privacy Officer at Prosus

Monika Tomczak-Górlikowska , Chief Privacy Officer at Prosus

Nils Beers , CEO at KickstartAI , co-founder and former CEO of TechLeap

Nils Beers , CEO at KickstartAI , co-founder and former CEO of TechLeap


--- Understanding the AI Act’s principles ---

Before the Act, the EU relied on voluntary guidelines to encourage responsible and trustworthy AI development. But as Jeannette Gorzala notes, many companies ignored them, resulting in severe harm.

“This is the first reason why we need the AI Act. To establish a minimum set of safety standards for AI development.” The second reason, she adds, is to eliminate legal uncertainty and prevent market fragmentation with the introduction of a single, unified AI rulebook .

Van Leeuwen, Director of AI at the Ministry of Justice and Security, shares a similar perspective. “The core of the AI act is making sure that we can be innovative whilst getting all these precautionary measures in place and adhering to our values and human rights, if there are risks attached to use cases. That's also the core of the thinking in the Netherlands."

The AI Act applies a horizontal, risk-based approach covering all AI systems being developed, provided, or used in the EU market. It defines four levels of risk , each with its own corresponding rules. Obligations increase in proportion to the risk level. The Act also includes a special set of rules for General Purpose AI (GPAI) models , which will come into force on August 2, 2025.

“I think we need to be clear that the maximum governance scope of the Act only impacts about 10 to 15% of AI use cases in Europe — those that fall under the high-risk category,” Gorzala says. She adds that all other use cases are minimally impacted, essentially subject to transparency requirements. For this reason, Gorzala advises companies to identify their position within the Act’s categories and start creating an AI roadmap.

From Van Leeuwen’s perspective, the EU’s tiered risk-based approach is a good way to look at artificial intelligence. “Not all types of AI are equally harmful or supportive,” he explains. But he acknowledges that this approach has also drawn criticism. “We made a law on an AI that’s not really here yet in all its fullest and final forms.”


--- Implementation challenges on the ground ---

A major concern voiced by many in the European tech sector is the regulation’s ambiguity.

The core of the risk categories is clear, according to Tomczak-Górlikowska, Chief Privacy Officer at Prosus. “But each of those categories contains a high number of descriptive elements that are subject to interpretation.” This makes it challenging for companies to assess whether they fall into the high-risk territory, which comes with the strictest obligations. Prosus, for instance, still doesn't have full clarity on which risk categories its various AI activities fall under.

Another source of complexity stems from how the law distinguishes between high-risk AI systems and GPAI models. “They’ve been treated separately, but in practice, things can quickly morph and the roles aren’t clearly cut,” Tomczak-Górlikowska says.

This means that companies need a constant monitoring and governance mechanism in place to ensure they’re still operating within your intended role, and to catch whether their business model has unintentionally shifted. To navigate the Act, she adds, companies need AI skills resources, risk management resources, as well as legal support.

For Van Leeuwen, who leads the implementation of the AI Act across the Ministry of Justice and Security, one of the challenging parts is deciding whether something constitutes AI or not based on the law’s definitions.

The Act includes some “open norms", he says, which he considers a positive element, as they enable the regulation to remain future-proof and adaptable to emerging technologies “But the flip side is that for the people who are making AI, it's not 100% clear in all of the situations how to make sure that you comply with this regulation.”

Adding to the challenges, Tomczak-Górlikowska points to the difficulty of navigating multiple regulations. “We have a number of laws in the digital space. They share similarities, but they also have their own twists and quirks. And the ability to simultaneously implement those at scale creates complexity.”

Nils Beers, CEO at KickstartAI, echoes this concern, hinting at the broader economic impact. “We have a large amount of rules and regulations, from the GDPR, to the DSA, the DMA, and now, the AI Act.”

While he acknowledges that the intention behind these laws is to uphold European values and safety , he warns that the outcome often tells a different story. “American companies get bigger, while European companies get smaller.”


--- Potential impact on innovation and competitiveness ---

According to Jeannette Gorzala, framing the AI Act as a trade-off between regulation and innovation is misleading. “I actually very much oppose this regulation-versus-innovation axis,” she says. “What we want is safe innovation — and for that, we need minimum safety standards.”

In her view, the AI act won’t stifle innovation, especially as the vast majority of AI use cases in the EU fall outside of the law’s scope. She also points to the Act’s pro-innovation measures. “It mandates that every member state must implement a regulatory AI sandbox, a space specifically designed to help innovators and entrepreneurs develop and test their AI applications.”

Tomczak-Górlikowska sees the sandboxes as a helpful mechanism for supporting innovation. Still, she cautions that they’re “only going to cover a subset of innovative solutions” and shouldn’t be seen as a fix for the broader tech ecosystem

One risk she highlights is that startups — especially those without the resources to manage compliance — may adapt their business models not to scale, but to avoid falling into the high-risk category. Similarly, the rules on GPAI models within the systemic risk group may also push companies away from cutting-edge work.

Beers illustrates how limiting that dynamic could be. “Imagine that you’re a small startup that can’t afford a legal team,” he says. “You will stop your development at least 40% before what is technically possible to make sure that you don’t fall into the high-risk area, which calls for hefty fines.”

This, according to Beers, creates a competitive disadvantage. Not only can big American or Chinese companies absorb any fines more easily, but they can also move faster and establish market dominance by being less bound by rules.

For investors weighing risk and return, Europe will also become less attractive, he adds. “Why would you invest in a company that’s ten times more likely to be regulated?”

“In theory, we’re trying to create responsible AI companies,” Beers says. “But in practice, we might end up making it harder for such companies to succeed, simply because they have to comply with a much higher standard compared to their American or Chinese counterparts.”

This, he argues, has wider consequences: Europe risks losing both its market and its sovereignty in the AI era.

Van Leeuwen acknowledges that regulation in general “always has some kind of decelerating effect.”

“There is a cost to regulatory compliance,” he says, “but if we agree that artificial intelligence will be one of the major drivers of societal change in the coming decades, it's impossible to imagine a reality where it won't be regulated.”


--- Call for support and open dialogue ---

Earlier in July, Prosus — alongside major European (and Dutch) companies including ASML, Philips, Cradle, and Mistral, led by the EU AI Champions initiative  — signed an open letter , calling the Commission for a two-year delay of the Act.

“We welcomed the call from the European Commission to provide our views on simplification of the EU AI Act and other digital laws — and we responded with concrete proposals,”  Tomczak-Górlikowska  explains.

“We signed the open letter not because we want the Act to go away for a while, but because we thought that a pause is a beneficial moment to assess some opportunities, simplify some of the requirements, and have a refreshed version aligned with the novel character of the technology and the advancements organizations have made in AI governance.”

The EU is expected to introduce its Digital Omnibus Package and Digital Fitness Check (aimed at streamlining and simplifying multiple digital laws) in the second half of 2025.

Beyond simplifying the AI Act’s requirements, Beers is also calling for greater support from national governments and the EU to help companies navigate the regulation, provide clear guidance, and offer financial assistance.

He argues that innovation-friendly regulation should be agile and fast, focused less on rigid processes and more on preventing actual harm.

“If you look at the vast majority of algorithms being applied today, 95% to 99% of the AI we’re using is designed to improve people’s lives,” he says. “It's about healthcare, it's about efficiency, it's about greening the economy, it's about reducing poverty.”

And precisely because of AI’s huge benefit to society , Beers believes that European companies have a strong negotiating power and the right to ask for regulation changes. “Imagine what would happen if we had an AI strike tomorrow,” he says.

“If we paused AI, if we closed data centers even for a couple of days, the whole society would come to a stop. I think it would show that AI isn’t a concept — it’s a fundamental part of our life, and if we change the rules around it, we need to make sure that we’re actually making things better.”

From the Dutch government's perspective, preparations for the AI Act are already underway, led by the Ministry of Economic Affairs. This includes setting up key oversight mechanisms, such as the national market surveillance authority and a single national contact point. At the same time, the government is investing in awareness and education initiatives — both within ministries and across the broader industry.

“We’re doing a lot of work not only to educate people about the AI Act, but also facilitating organizations in compliance, and invest in ongoing deliberations with Brussels on specifying certain open elements in the regulation,” Van Leeuwen says.

=== Community Spotlight: Niya Stoimenova ===


--- Why calling LLMs 'reasoning' models is misleading ---

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

Every month, we spotlight one member for our community . At KickstartAI, we believe that meaningful progress starts with individuals — builders, researchers, founders, and practitioners — who are applying AI in bold, practical ways. Our Community Spotlight series celebrates their work, their challenges, and the lessons they’re learning along the way.

This month, we’re featuring Niya Stoimenova , AI Product & Strategic Foresight Lead at DEUS. With a background spanning mathematics, philosophy, design as well as a PhD in AI reasoning, Niya is focusing on driving enterprise-wide responsible AI transformation.

Over the past year, leading AI companies have marketed a number of their latest LLMs as “reasoning” models, claiming performance that competes with PhD-level experts in coding and math. Think of OpenAI’s GPT-5,  o3 and o4-mini models, or Anthropic’s Claude 3.7 Sonnet.

But according to Niya Stoimenova, who holds a PhD in AI reasoning , this labeling is more than hype — it’s misleading.

During her PhD research, Niya developed a first-of-its-kind theoretical model that maps out the different modes of reasoning one needs to generate novel ideas. She then applied her framework to examine how current AI models can be used to also do that.

She found that, while AI systems do have certain types of reasoning, they’re missing a very important kind.

“In order to generate novel insights in general, we make use of three main categories of reasoning,” she says. “Deduction and induction we all know. And then we have a different type of reasoning, abduction, that humans rely on to make explanations and develop new ideas. But that’s missing in AI.”

This missing link, she argues, makes a big difference not only in what AI can do, but also in how we perceive it. And that’s more than just a matter of semantics.


--- The types of reasoning LLMs possess ---

According to Niya, deduction, induction, and abduction, all come together to enable a complete reasoning process, or what we perceive as thinking.

Deduction in the ability to apply a known rule in order to reach a certain conclusion. She provides a very simple example: We know that water boils at 100°C. We see that water is boiling in the pan, and we deduce that its temperature must be 100°C.

But if we don’t know what the water’s boiling temperature is, we can apply induction, the ability to create rules from a set of observations or patterns. We measure water temperature when it starts to boil, and note that it’s 100°C. We repeat this many times and we induce the general rule: “Water boils at 100°C.”

“Most of machine learning is a combination of deduction and induction,” Niya explains.

“What LLMs, specifically, are doing is a large-scale induction,” she adds. “They generalize patterns from vast amounts of data and then they try to predict the patterns based on what they've already seen. They're doing induction with some forms of deduction.”

Deduction and induction are both types of reasoning, Niya says. But this doesn’t necessarily mean that, by possessing them, LLMs can reason at the level needed to reach AGI. “Your calculator is doing deduction. So by the industry’s definition, your calculator is also a reasoning model.”

“Even if you look into OpenAI’s o3, or into some of Anthropic's reasoning models, what they're doing is using chain-of-thought reasoning. They list different types of steps and execute them.”

But that’s not the type of reasoning that generates novel insights, according to Niya. To do that, they would need abduction.


--- Why missing abductive reasoning matters ---

In its simplest form, (explanatory) abduction is the ability to generate explanations or hypotheses using very limited information.

Niya provides another example: You walk outside and you see the street is wet. Since you’re in the Netherlands, you assume it probably rained, although it might have actually been that someone cleaned the street. Or a pipe burst.

Although this reasoning isn’t reliable, it helps us navigate everyday situations by identifying the most plausible explanations. Niya argues that the industry confuses this type of abduction with inference to the best explanation (IBE), although IBE is still a form of induction.

“And when it comes to actually generating novel insights and ideas, then we’re talking about innovative abduction,” she adds. “It works like this: you don’t know what exactly you want to do or how you’re going to do it, but you know why. And there are certain mechanisms humans use to get from that ambiguous “why” to novel solutions.”

“This is the type of reasoning we need in order to get to general artificial intelligence, and deliver on the promises that AI will create new drugs and invent many different things.”

Niya emphasizes that new ideas and concepts come from abductive reasoning, while deduction and induction play supporting roles by testing and validating them.


--- The risks of mislabeling LLMs’ capabilities ---

In Niya’s view, reasoning as a whole comes together and leads to novelty when we employ all its different types. This is why she finds the labeling of LLMs as “reasoning” models misleading.

“A lot of people don't really understand what type of reasoning the model is providing to them,” she says, which means that they might misinterpret their capabilities.

“What LLMs generate seems very plausible, and can even feel novel, even though there is very little novelty in it,” she explains. “And while their answers seem to be true, it doesn't necessarily mean they are. This comes from the largely inductive nature of LLMs. So,unless you have very deep knowledge of a subject, it’s very difficult to catch mistakes or hallucinations.”

The way LLMs display confidence adds to the problem: they sound convincing. “Unfortunately, I think not too many people know how to properly manage the pitfalls of these models, precisely because LLMs are so useful.”

Niya adds that this becomes even more challenging when implementing them into automated workflows and day-to-day operations. “It’s not enough to just give people access to Copilot or ChatGPT. You need education and you need to rethink a lot of parameters on how your organization operates."

“How do you actually design the workflows? How do you talk about organizational processes? How do you understand outcomes? How do you define roles? And how do you actually put these models into existing systems, or redesign them?”

Ultimately, she argues, value won’t come from the models alone, but from the systems we build around them. “You need proper guardrails,” Niya says. “And you need to create a blend of human and LLM-augmented type of interaction.”

=== ING’s approach to innovating wholesale banking with AI ===

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

AI is becoming integral to modern banking. From advanced fraud detection to chatbots and personalized services, it now supports both front- and back-office operations, providing financial institutions with a competitive advantage.

As adoption expands, wholesale banking represents a critical frontier for AI — one defined by large-scale transactions, complex products, and regulatory pressure. To understand how this shift is unfolding, we take a closer look at how ING is incorporating AI into its wholesale operations.

We spoke with two executives from ING:

Kacy Harding , Chief Product Officer, WB Analytics

Kacy Harding , Chief Product Officer, WB Analytics

Bahadir Yilmaz , Chief Analytics Officer

Bahadir Yilmaz , Chief Analytics Officer

They provide insights into early and emerging use cases, how ING evaluates opportunities and measures success, the challenges of AI development, and the vision for where the technology could ultimately take the sector.


--- AI's role in wholesale banking ---

For a global bank like ING, the integration of AI is not just a technological upgrade; it's a fundamental shift in how operations are managed and how clients are served. “At ING, we see generative AI not just as a technology — but as a catalyst for transformation,” says Yilmaz.

“In wholesale banking, we’ve moved beyond experimentation to delivering real, scalable impact across client services, lending, and sustainability," he notes. "From automating customer due diligence and enhancing ESG insights to launching our WB Client Services Chatbot, GenAI is helping our teams work smarter and serve clients better.”

Harding sees AI as a “critical enabler” that allows the bank to seamlessly leverage large amounts of data quickly and in a scalable way.

The need for speed and scalability is inherent to wholesale banking, which caters to large clients such as corporates. “We’re dealing with highly complex data,” Harding explains. A key example is regulation, as financial rules and institutional structures vary widely across countries and sectors.

“AI really helps us churn through information and identify the pieces we need to serve our clients,” she says. It also enables ING to “piggybank” on the years of experience and domain knowledge of its front- and back-office experts.

“We work together with the front and back office on how to interpret and process data, and how to build the AI models,” Harding notes. Thanks to such benefits, she highlights that AI is driving the move from incremental improvements in analytics to breakthrough innovation.


--- Three AI use cases ---


>>> Know Your Customer (KYC) <<<

Know Your Customer (KYC) is essential for verifying client identities and protecting society and financial institutions against risks such as fraud and money laundering. It’s also “a highly manual process, and one that needs to be repeated regularly,” she notes.

That’s why ING has turned to generative AI to streamline the Customer Due Diligence (CDD) side of the process. By gathering data from unstructured source documents, GenAI can reduce touch time, manual file handling, and alert handling — enabling analysts to spend less time on data collection and more on customer analysis and outreach.

Launched on July 1st of this year, a GenAI project providing CDD transaction profile reports has already demonstrated its value in practice. It saves front-office analysts an average of two hours per client and is now in use across all 19 countries where ING operates.


>>> ESG insights <<<

For ING, a key element for loan approvals in wholesale banking is the verification of a client’s ESG profile.

“As part of this process,” Harding says, “we need to conduct multiple compliance checks, ensure all required questions have been asked, and verify the accuracy of documents such as climate reports and credit applications.” One of the main challenges is that documents come in different formats, from texts and images to tables.

To improve efficiency, she explains, ING has been using the support of Large Language Models (LLMs). “They extract relevant information from those documents and transfer them into a system, where our colleagues can verify that we followed all of the compliance steps, and that we are able to verify and audit that we have cited that information from our client.”

Harding notes that AI has been instrumental in streamlining document processing, reducing an average time of six hours to a few seconds.


>>> Centralizing knowledge <<<

Harding’s team is currently experimenting with LLMs that can help centralize knowledge for internal use. The primary goal is to provide back-office experts with a single, up-to-date, accurate source of information.

“We want to make our internal content more accessible and easier to interpret, so that a service agent can quickly and concisely synthesize relevant information and respond more effectively,” Harding says. She believes that this will also have a significant impact in client service and customer engagement, as it will be possible to cross-reference information related to the specific conditions of a particular customer.

“We’re starting small and I do anticipate challenges around hallucinations,” she says. The team keeps testing the model and challenges it with wrong answers or difficult questions to evaluate its performance.

“So far, it hasn’t hallucinated, which makes us more confident that we’re following the right approach.”


--- Behind the curtain ---


>>> Identifying potential use cases <<<

“Our philosophy is to democratize the inclusion of different challenges from across the wholesale banking department,” Harding says. This means that any employee can submit particular issues or challenges they’re experiencing. These submissions are then flagged against key business objectives.

At this point, a dedicated team called “Discovery Squad” takes over. The squad reviews the highest-priority challenges through a defined vetting process. They define the problem, assess its technical feasibility and business viability, and secure risk approval. Then follows the proof-of-concept development process.

“One of the critical criteria we put in place when making the high-priority problems list is whether we can have access to healthy and clean data,” Harding says. “If we don’t, but still believe we want to work on a particular use case, we need to ensure that, if there is a data gap, those attributes aren’t something that can throw the algorithm off, or bias our results.”


>>> Measuring the success of AI <<<

To evaluate the success of AI solutions, ING uses three main metrics, starting with  quantitative metrics. These include the projected cost savings and efficiency gains, AI’s expected accuracy in solving the problem, and the capital investment needed.

“With AI, as with any breakthrough innovation, it’s more challenging to balance your evaluation scorecard,” Harding says. “We need to make a high upfront capital investment in order to achieve cost and efficiency savings.”

On the qualitative side is employee feedback. “This will also include client feedback when we get to the stage that our products are live on the market,” Harding adds.

The third metric has to do with regulatory audits. “Can we ensure that there is transparency in what we're developing? Are we able to prove the answers that the model is coming up with are accurate? And are our systems compliant?”


--- Challenges ---

According to Harding, there are multiple layers of complexity, starting with regulatory compliance. “There are requirements both per region and per country, which also change depending on the sector you’re working in,” she says. “You really have to be on top of your game in understanding how to work within the system.”

In addition, beyond the data quality itself as an underlying source of how the products and models are built, there is also the challenge of identity and access management. “We need to make sure that sensitive information and results can only be viewed by the approved people and roles within the organization.”

At the core of AI development is also the requirement for transparency, auditability, accountability, and explainability. “This underpins the entire process, from how the model has been built to how it performs and what kind of results are being generated,” Harding says.


--- Towards optimal action prediction and agentic AI ---

Regardless of the challenges, Harding shares her enthusiasm about AI future potential in banking. She believes that as we experience the tangible benefits of AI and build greater trust in the technology, its role will shift from driving efficiency and competitive advantage to enabling deep personalization and automating complex financial processes.

“Just as analytics evolved from looking at historical data to suggesting the next best action,” she says, “AI will leap forward to more predictions and recommendations of optimal actions across the entire client journey.” For example: what is most optimal for a specific client, in a particular sector, under certain market conditions.

This requires models that are transparent and explainable, but also context-aware — integrating internal data with external sources, such as competitive benchmarks, market shifts, or regulatory developments.

“I think that could be a very exciting next phase,” Harding says.

“As we enter the next chapter, our ambition is clear: to expand GenAI’s reach, embrace agentic AI for front-office productivity, and continue building responsibly — with strong governance, auditable systems, and a relentless focus on customer value,” adds Yilmaz.

--- How data for good projects can shape a data scientist ---

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

Every month, we spotlight one member for our community . At KickstartAI, we believe that meaningful progress starts with individuals — builders, researchers, founders, and practitioners — who are applying AI in bold, practical ways. Our Community Spotlight series celebrates their work, their challenges, and the lessons they’re learning along the way.

This month, we’re featuring Anastasiia Kulakova , Data Scientist II at Kraken and volunteer at CorrelAid. From applying data science in the energy sector to contributing to data for good projects in the nonprofit world, this is her story.

This outlook guides both her professional and personal life. At Kraken, Anastasiia uses applied data science for the sustainable energy sector. Outside of work, she’s an active supporter of the Data4Good movement — a wide initiative that applies data science for societal benefit rather than profit, from tackling climate change to supporting human rights among other causes.

Anastasiia is a volunteer at CorrelAid , a non-profit network of data scientists who aim to advance social good. Volunteering has long been a big part of her life, she says, and she first started contributing to different projects during her university studies.

It was when she attended a CorrelAid meetup in 2022 that she realized volunteering didn’t necessarily mean offering hours or manual labor. “You could also offer the skills you developed through your education and work training. I thought it was a perfect match!” she recalls.


--- Matching refugees with welcome groups ---

Anastasiia’s first project with CorrelAid was carried out for the Justice & Peace non-profit organization in the Hague. The goal was to match people with refugee backgrounds to welcome groups: long-term residents or Dutch citizens who could help newcomers navigate language barriers, institutions, and bureaucracy.

To support this, the CorrelAid team built a proper database, where applications from welcome groups and newcomers could be stored and later used for algorithmic matching. “Good matches” could be defined in many ways, but geographical proximity was a clear factor, Anastasiia explains.

“I contributed to the foundational part of data engineering, setting up the infrastructure,” she explains. “The nonprofit had previously relied on surveys and manual input in different formats, with no existing databases.”


--- Battling homelessness ---

Another project , which Anastasiia participated in, focused on helping people who were economically homeless — those unable to afford housing — by placing them in spare hotel rooms.

Hotels could offer vacancies free of charge, giving people a temporary place to stay, sometimes just long enough to prepare for a job interview or take first steps toward employment.

The project was less about advanced data science and more about data management and storytelling, Anastasiia explains, to support the goals of CorrelAid’s partner organization.

“The first goal was operational efficiency: much of their work was done manually, and without a single source of ‘truth’ they couldn’t effectively identify whether hotel rooms were available or not.” The second goal, she adds, was to raise funding. “They wanted to communicate the project’s positive impact in numbers and create a compelling visualization for donors.”


--- How data for good projects can change a data scientist’s perspective ---

“Working on data for good projects, especially as part of a nonprofit, has made me think more end-to-end in my approach,” Anastasiia notes, “seeing how my work connects to teams, end products, and the wider context.”

She adds that with nonprofits usually being financially strained, their people wear many hats, are multidisciplinary and multifunctional. “You gradually learn the same type of attitude. It’s likely that in your day job, you’re tied to a particular function or specialization — but in a nonprofit you learn how to be a generalist. We often hear volunteers say that the experience gave them the confidence to take more initiative at work and step outside their comfort zones.”

Volunteering at CorrelAid has also shifted how Anastasiia thinks about budgets. “As techies, we love shiny new things, but now I try to measure cost vs. impact, even in the commercial setting of my company, where funding is less of a problem.” She’s also more cautious in how she communicates at work, making sure to back her claims with data — a habit shaped by the transparency demands of nonprofit projects.

One of Anastasiia’s dreams is to make data for good volunteering more mainstream. The hurdle she sees is that, often, such projects seem less exciting from a technical perspective.

“A lot of us are technology geeks. We like to play with LLMs. We like to play with fast computes and new dashboards. But nonprofits are usually less technologically mature organizations, which may need help with setting up the data infrastructure, or creating their first database.”

But even these small things, she notes, can have a big impact. “Even though we are technical people, we still want to feel the impact of what we’re doing. In bigger organizations sometimes it takes time to deliver this impact, or it’s broken down into many roles and departments, so it seems small,” she says. But in nonprofits, it’s possible to see results very quickly. “There is nothing, and then there is something!”


--- Advice to data scientists ---

Anastasiia’s advice to data scientists is to learn by doing. “Start with a pet project, set a milestone you want to achieve, but don’t aim for perfection immediately.”

A common challenge, she says, is falling too deeply into knowledge gaps. The key is deciding “what’s good enough” to move forward and incrementally make improvements and build features.

She also encourages participation in communities and meetups. “They are essential for seeing what other tools are there. Maybe you come across one that’s interesting, or that sounds relevant to your problem. You pick it up, you try to work with it, and once you’re comfortable with it, it ends up in your toolbox.”

=== AI in education: how the technology is tested in Dutch schools ===

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

AI is no longer just an abstract concept in education — it’s being tested in classrooms through concrete projects. In the Netherlands, the National Education Lab AI (NOLAI)  brings together researchers, schools, and teachers to explore how AI can meaningfully support learning and teaching.

At the center of this effort are three projects focusing on secondary education: tools that help students with self-regulation while writing, semi-automated scoring systems, and technologies that track student progress and recommend learning tasks.

Each of these projects addresses a pressing challenge in schools today, from reducing teacher workload to helping students take ownership of their learning. Together, they offer a window into what AI can — and cannot or should not — do for education.

We spoke with three experts involved in these projects:

Joep van der Graaf, Assistant Professor at NOLAI & the Behavioural Science Institute (BSI) at Radboud University

Joep van der Graaf, Assistant Professor at NOLAI & the Behavioural Science Institute (BSI) at Radboud University

Eva de Schipper , Educational Researcher at CitoLab

Eva de Schipper , Educational Researcher at CitoLab

Johan Jeuring , Professor of Software Technology of Learning and Teaching at Utrecht University & Chief Scientific Officer at DialogueTrainer

Johan Jeuring , Professor of Software Technology of Learning and Teaching at Utrecht University & Chief Scientific Officer at DialogueTrainer


--- Self-regulation while writing ---

Launched in 2023, the project aims to improve students’ writing skills by developing a solution that supports self-regulation — the ability to monitor and direct one’s own learning process.

The project focuses on essay writing for six courses (geography, biology, history, Dutch, and English) in collaboration with teachers from Candea College in Duiven.

“We target self-regulated learning and we hope to see effects in the writing product,” says Joep van der Graaf, Assistant Professor at NOLAI, Radboud University, and scientific lead. To do this, the team developed a student dashboard which combines two main tools: an algorithm to track students’ learning process and NLP models that evaluate the essays.

Participating students work in a custom learning environment and complete a series of five lessons, two of which include essay writing followed by reflection.

A key component of the project is the Flora algorithm, which translates raw trace data (mouse clicks, keystrokes) into meaningful actions and learning patterns.

“If you click on a button to go to the instruction page, this is recorded as a simple click. The action label would be ‘going to the instruction page.’ Based on the action labels, we determine patterns of actions,” van der Graaf says. These patterns correspond to different learning processes, such as orientation and planning.

The dashboard shows students their actions as interpreted by the algorithm, which they can then use to reflect on their work.

"We started with a theoretical framework from the educational sciences that describes how the learning process happens in self-regulated learning,” van der Graaf says. The team then combined think-aloud and trace data and applied the Detect, Diagnose, Act framework.

While there were available coding schemes based on self-regulated learning theory to label think-aloud data, no such scheme for trace data existed at the time. “We developed it ourselves, ensuring it closely resembled the think-aloud scheme. We wanted to capture the same processes so we could also evaluate the labeling algorithm’s performance. And then we ran a comparison.”

“We identified some gaps based on the data, but we also saw some additional patterns that could be related to a process. We fine-tuned the algorithm and did another test, achieving a match rate of about 55%.”

Van der Graaf explains that the writing evaluation feature was added later on, following feedback that students weren’t engaged enough in reflecting about the learning process alone.

The NLP models evaluate the content of the essays rather than the language and style. “We use three criteria provided to us by the teachers,” he says. “One of them is capturing the main points. We currently use NLP for history and biology.”

“Students can also see on the dashboard’s timeline at which part of the learning process (orientation, reading, or writing for instance) they covered one of the main points.” This way, they can understand how their entire writing process connects to their writing product.

Van der Graaf provides an example: “A student might reflect, ‘I didn’t plan enough and missed most of the main points — I should try a different approach next time.’ From there, they can set goals for the next essay, such as planning more reading time. If they follow through, it’s possible their essay will be better the next time.”

The project is currently in the validation phase, and the team is working on a prototype teacher dashboard.


--- Semi-automated grading support ---

On the teacher support front, CitoLab and NOLAI are working together on CheckMate : a digital grading environment that helps grading short, open-ended answers to test questions.

“We started researching the topic and built a prototype already in 2022,” says Eva de Schipper, Educational Researcher at Cito, who’s also leading the CheckMate project . In 2024, CitoLab joined a NOLAI co-creation project aiming to further develop  the prototype with teachers from two schools.

The goal of the project is to address two main grading bottlenecks identified by teachers: the time-consuming nature of scoring and consistency in terms of fairness and accuracy.

De Schipper explains how CheckMate works: students see a question on the screen and type their answers into a text box. Once all answers are submitted, the system reviews them, groups similar responses, highlights key words, and suggests scores using different colors.

These suggestions show how other teachers evaluated similar answers, while the system also learns from a teacher’s own grading and adapts its recommendations accordingly.

“The teachers are still in charge of the final score and they don’t need to accept the suggestions ,” she says.

“We’re focusing on biology and geography courses for now, because the answers are shorter and there aren’t too many numericals,” de Schipper adds.

The team is currently working further on the clustering algorithm that groups student answers to also perform well in longer responses. “We feel it contributes the most to easing the burden of teachers, and we have received unanimously positive feedback.”

While parts of how CheckMate operates might change, the prototype is currently using NLP to process answers. “We first do pre-processing for the student answers to keep the parts of the responses that are relevant for scoring content quality,” de Schipper explains.

“Imagine if a student wrote down ‘the forest’. ‘Forest’ is a key component of the answer, but ‘the’ isn’t relevant. Similarly, a spelling mistake isn’t a problem when measuring their knowledge of biology.”

The team applies the same pre-processing technique for the one, or more, right answers in the scoring model.

"We then use vectors for the words in the answers, taken from an open-source language model. From these, we create an average representation of the meaning of a student’s answer and compare it to both the right answers and the answers of other students that have already been scored.”

This way, the system combines both reference-based and peer-based scoring.

According to de Schipper, one of the reasons behind choosing NLP is transparency. “We can precisely tell to users why we gave the score we did,” she says, adding that isn’t the case with generative AI.

Another reason is language. While generative AI models have strong scoring performance on large-scale English datasets, their performance in Dutch isn’t yet at the same level. A third reason is GenAI’s environmental impact.

According to de Schipper, CheckMate is able to score a large number of student answers with high certainty. “For biology in vocational secondary education, our scoring agreement with the first grader is around 95%,” she says. But she also notes this accuracy is a somewhat elusive concept.

“Accuracy is only defined in comparison to what teachers do, and what we know from the data is that teachers don't always agree with each other even on the same precise student answers.”

“What is the golden standard? That's one of the biggest questions for us,” she says. Another big question is to what extent scoring should be automated. De Schipper says that teacher feedback is so far mixed and ranges between semi- and full automation.

The project just completed the concept phase. The team will now start working on a renewed version of the prototype, incorporating teacher feedback. De Schipper hopes that, once the project finishes in 2027, the lessons from CheckMate can have widespread adoption.


--- Progress tracking and task recommendations for courses ---

Teacher shortages are another area where AI is helping provide (at least temporary) solutions. One such example is Co-Teach Informatica , a national program launched four years ago to address the lack of computer science teachers in secondary education.

Through the Co-Teach platform, students can still take computer science classes using fully online teaching materials, with class management support from teachers of other subjects, and learning support at a distance from universities. As a follow-up, a complementary NOLAI project addresses the challenge of setting appropriate learning goals and recommending suitable learning tasks without a certified computer teacher in the classroom.

“We’re focusing on tracking student progress and recommending tasks within a course,” explains Johan Jeuring — Professor of Software Technology for Learning and Teaching at Utrecht University — who’s also leading the research within the project.

The aim is to identify potential knowledge gaps before the end-of-chapter course exams, and remedy them by suggesting additional tasks.

At the core of the system lies an individual student model, which tracks a learner’s progress on knowledge and skills. “To track student progress, we annotated all tasks within a course with the learning goals they are addressing, such as the correct usage of the if-then-else statement,” Jeuring says.

As students complete tasks, their performance is recorded: some tasks are automatically assessed, while others involve self-assessment against a model solution. The results update the student model, allowing the system to monitor and analyze progress toward the set learning goals, and recommend suitable learning materials.

The team developed its solution using bayesian knowledge tracing — an established method for intelligent tutoring systems, which models a student’s knowledge using performance data and probabilities.

“We calculate the probability that a student knows how to solve a particular task,” Jeuring explains. “If a student has a good result, the probability that they have mastered the subject increases. If they make an error, the probability decreases.”

They system updates accordingly a student’s knowledge network. Jeuring provides an example: suppose a student writes a correct if–then–else statement. Since this also requires the use of Boolean conditions, the system increases not only the probability of mastering if–then–else statements but also Booleans.

One remaining challenge is determining how effective the recommendations are, which the team plans to do in the final year of the project as it enters the validation phase.

“It is a non-trivial problem, because it’s not always guaranteed that the algorithm’s recommendations align with what students need or expect. What we do know is that if a student fails certain tasks, the system will recommend related tasks.”

“The harder question is if practicing those tasks helps the student improve and understand the material better. This is something we still need to look at.”

According to Jeuring, their solution is a platform technology. “There's nothing that makes it specific for computer science and actually the people who developed the Co-Teach platform are now also using it for some mathematics courses.”

The team is currently piloting its system in two schools, and Jeuring hopes that they will soon be able to roll out their results to more schools and courses.


--- Building AI in education: opportunities and risks ---

Beyond the benefits that personalization and adaptive learning can bring, Joep van der Graaf also sees AI as a way to create new kinds of educational situations that were previously impossible. “With our technology, for example, we can now track the learning process of 30 students at a time.”

Eva de Schipper emphasizes the potential of AI to support teachers more directly. She notes that teachers often need a more complete and up-to-date picture of their students — not only in individual subjects but also across subjects and on a more personal level.

For Johan Jeuring, the key opportunity of AI in education is its ability to collect student knowledge and use this information to provide teachers with insights into student needs for further progress.

However, he cautions that we should always be aware of data unfairness and bias. “Each time you learn something from data, you learn it from data from a particular group of students or a particular level and as soon as you use it for another group, it might not be representative any more.”

He also expresses concerns about the unrestricted use of generative AI by students, which might negatively affect the learning process by removing the difficulty one should experience to learn.

Van der Graaf shares a similar perspective. Citing a colleague , he refers to the risk of “metacognitive laziness.” When students rely on generative AI for good results, they stop thinking through tasks themselves and lose sight of what they are learning. The focus narrows to execution, leaving them unsure how to approach similar tasks on their own.

“As educational scientists, we should also keep in mind these questions: what do we want the future generation to learn? And what do we think is relevant?” he adds. The answers will define which learning goals — and at which stage — should remain independent of AI.

For Eva de Schipper, adopting AI in education calls for caution.“The introduction of  automated scoring tools or scoring assistance into the educational environment already has so much potential to make an impact. But we don’t know what that impact will be and how it will change the didactics of school, or how it will be for students, parents, and all relevant stakeholders.”

=== From idea to impact: lessons on how to implement AI in large organizations ===


--- The Erasmus MC case ---

By Sophia Zitman & Evertjan Peer

Many AI projects start with big ideas and impressive proofs-of-concept, but often fail before they create any real value inside a large organization. The barrier is rarely the technology. It’s making the leap from a pilot to a solution that fits the organization’s actual processes, strategy, and people.

At KickstartAI, our focus is on closing this implementation gap. A recent project with Erasmus MC — where we built a system to predict patient inflow — in their busy emergency department, offers clear lessons on what it really takes to move AI from idea to impact.


--- The initial spark vs the real need ---

The project began with a clear technical proposal: build a model that predicts patient number arrivals in the ER so staff could manage capacity better. On paper, it was a solid idea — technically sound and tied to an obvious need.

But experience has taught us that many AI projects stall when they start with technology first. A clever model alone rarely delivers lasting impact. That’s why, right from the beginning, we asked the kinds of questions that make or break large-scale AI initiatives. And these aren’t just “nice to have.” They’re essential to making sure a solution is sustainable, valuable, and actually used.

💡TIP: Our approach goes beyond just asking questions in meetings. We shadow staff, observe their workflows, and sometimes even step into the work ourselves. This hands-on approach grounds our understanding in reality, validates assumptions, and surfaces the things that really matter.


--- Lesson 1: Invest time in defining the business problem ---

When Erasmus MC first came to us, the question on the table was: how can we reduce the moments the ER goes on “red” — reaching maximum capacity and unable to safely accept new patients?

It was a real challenge, but also extremely broad. “Going red” could be triggered by many different things, such as patient inflow, discharge bottlenecks, room availability, or staffing shortages.

That’s why we began with a dedicated 3-4 month “Use Case Identifier” phase before any intensive model development began. The goal wasn’t to write code, but to understand the problem deeply from the hospital’s perspective.

Stakeholder interviews

Talking to everyone involved — from frontline staff to management — surfaced one key insight: the central role of the managing nurse. This person is the operational hub of the ER, deciding which nurse takes which patient, when to redirect patients elsewhere in the hospital, and how to keep flow moving.

Walking the floor

We shadowed staff and asked them to explain their work “as if to a new colleague,” making sure nothing obvious was left unsaid.  We saw firsthand how big of a task it  was for managing nurses to make capacity decisions with incomplete information. They were the ones truly “feeling the pain.” Mapping the process

With the workflows laid out, we could identify the key decision points and constraints. It became clear where there was room to maneuver — such as staffing patterns and patient routing — and where there were hard limits. Mapping also revealed exactly where timely information could make a difference, and who needed it most.

This deep dive refined the objective from a vague ambition (“avoid going red”) to a concrete solution: building a model that predicts patient inflow to help managing nurses make proactive decisions.

Together with the nurses, we discovered that short-term predictions (half an hour ahead) were too late to act on, while long-range ones (six hours out) were too uncertain. The sweet spot was a 1-4 hour window: short enough to be reliable, long enough to take action.

💡TIP: That upfront time investment turned a broad challenge into a specific, actionable tool designed to support managing nurses directly, while tying into the hospital’s strategic goal of reducing ER overcrowding.


--- Lesson 2: Assess feasibility beyond the algorithm ---

Feasibility isn’t just about whether you can build an algorithm. It’s about whether the solution works in the real world.

Data availability & quality

Do we have the necessary data, and is it reliable and fit for our purpose? It’s key to testing assumptions about what kinds of data are actually useful.

In the Erasmus MC case, weather data seemed like an obvious predictor — nurses even suggested it: when it freezes, cyclists fall and ER visits rise. But when we linked historical weather patterns to patient inflow, the data told a different story. Erasmus MC focuses on severe, complex trauma, not everyday accidents like a broken wrist from a bike fall. Such cases are directed to other hospitals.

Predictive power vs. uncertainty

How accurate can the prediction realistically be, and how much inherent uncertainty exists in the process? ER patient flow has significant inherent variability, for example. Acknowledging these limitations early and designing ways to manage uncertainty (like providing prediction ranges or scenarios) was key to keeping the tool practical and trustworthy. Technical integration

Can the solution be embedded into the existing IT infrastructure? And who owns these systems?

Erasmus MC had experience with AI, but mostly in isolated environments for very specific medical use cases. Our project needed to run on a hospital-wide system and support daily operations.

That meant pulling together expertise from multiple departments. Different teams knew parts of the puzzle — machine learning lifecycles, data pipelines, system administration — but rarely had they been connected in a single  project. The challenge was to design something that worked immediately without being blocked by unfinished projects, while still aligning with where the hospital’s infrastructure was heading. That called for balancing speed with future-proofing, and thinking carefully about long-term ownership and maintenance.

💡TIP: Ignoring any of these factors leads to solutions that work in a lab but never make it into practice.


--- Lesson 3: Plan for adoption from day one ---

An AI solution only creates value when people actually use it — consistently, and within their daily workflows. Adoption isn’t about dropping a new tool on someone’s desk; it has to be built into the process from the very beginning. This involves four steps:

User interface & experience

What’s the best way to present AI’s output so it feels intuitive and actionable for end users?

We involved nurses in designing the interface from day one. In early sessions, we asked them to explain how they would present the information themselves when onboarding a new colleague, for example. Some leaned toward color systems, others preferred graphs, and some chose simple tables. These differences gave us valuable clues about what felt natural. We then created mock-ups in several formats and tested them again. The focus wasn’t on aesthetics but on actionability and readability: could nurses quickly digest the information, and did it help them take the right actions?

Interpretation & transparency

How should staff understand the AI’s output, including its uncertainties and limitations? Transparency is key, especially around prediction ranges and confidence levels. Taking the tim to explain to staff how to read and use these signals is also necessary to ensure that the system isn’t just functional but also trusted. Process change

What existing processes need to adapt, and what new actions should be triggered by the AI’s insights? This meant looking closely at how predictions would slot into nurses’ existing routines.

Building enthusiasm & sustaining adoption

A major benefit of involving end-users early is the feeling of inclusion.

Delivering a solution “out of the blue” would have felt like a surprise. Instead, involving nurses from the start helped create enthusiasm, foster early adopters, and build ambassadors who could spread the word among colleagues.

As we later prepared for rollout, we also thought about how to sustain adoption. One idea was peer-to-peer sharing: for instance, giving nurses a stage in weekly meetings to talk about how they use the tool. 💡TIP: Thinking about adoption from day one — and making it visible along the way — is what turns a technical project into something that sticks. Start small, involve users closely, and you create the conditions for trust, enthusiasm, and long-term use.


--- Conclusion: Weaving AI into the organizational fabric ---

Successfully implementing AI in large organizations takes a holistic approach. It starts with understanding the problem and operational context, checking what’s actually feasible with the data and systems in place, and planning early for adoption.

The Erasmus MC project showed that success when AI isn’t treated as a standalone tool, but as part of the hospital’s fabric — tied to its strategy, processes, and people. That’s what turns a promising idea into something that provides tangible impact day to day.

=== How Europe's network of open-source LLMs is evolving ===

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

In the global race for AI development, the EU has been following a distinctive approach: open-source Large Language Models (LLMs).

The objective is clear: firstly, to create models that are transparent in their design and training data, aligning with the bloc’s commitment to trustworthy and responsible AI. Secondly, to release such models freely for anyone to build their AI applications and foster collaboration. And thirdly, to develop multilingual LLMs that serve the Union’s linguistic and cultural diversity.

Examples of this approach include two EU co-funded initiatives: EuroLLM , which offers 9B and 1.7B parameter models, and OpenEuroLLM which is working on a series of foundation models.

“Over the next year, we’ll start seeing the results of these projects,” says Edwin Rijgersberg, the creator of GEITje — the first open Dutch LLM — and founder of the AI Studio Delta , who spoke at TechDays this year.

“But if you look at what’s been happening lately, it’s mostly about national initiatives,” he adds. These include France’s LUCIE , Spain’s ALIA models , and the Netherlands’ (albeit not fully open-source) GPT-NL . “Germany is also very active,” he says.


--- Different paths, shared European values ---

All these initiatives share key European principles: they’re (almost exclusively) open-source, transparent, and linguistically inclusive. Yet, they also demonstrate differences in both development and vision.

Perhaps the most unique of the current national efforts is the Dutch GPT-NL project, according to Rijgersberg. Now nearing its completion, GPT-NL stands out with its commitment to using strictly opt-in data, released under permissive licenses, donated voluntarily, or provided through partnerships.

“This is an interesting experiment and one that Europe can learn from. But it’s also a very challenging strategy to execute, especially for a language like Dutch that already doesn’t have much available text data to train on,” Rijgersberg says.

That challenge doesn’t just affect training — it also shapes how open the model can be. Under its opt-in data approach, GPT-NL’s source code and openly licensed datasets will be made public, but the model weights and copyrighted material will remain behind research and professional licences.

Meanwhile, across Europe, countries are forming their own visions for AI. France, for instance, “is pouring a lot of money and attention into what I call ‘creating’ AI,” Rijgersberg says, “rejecting the idea that they have to rely on the US or China.” By contrast, the Netherlands’ focus is mainly on the correct and ethical use of generative AI applications, rather than on building the underlying GenAI models.

Still, Rijgersberg sees a critical benefit in this diversity of approaches: “All these country-level efforts gather data and experience that is shared all over Europe.”

“Countries don’t have to reinvent the wheel by themselves.”


--- Towards a pan-European open-source network ---

Performance-wise, European open-source LLMs do not yet match the best American and Chinese models, which Rijgersberg attributes to two primary reasons: the lack of computational power and the need to comply with the EU’s text and data mining rules.

“If you look at benchmarks, with all these smaller initiatives, we have surpassed the performance of Meta’s Llama 2. I would say that we are approximately about a year behind, which is still a million years in AI.”

But Rijgersberg remains optimistic, describing this as a “surmountable gap,” especially as the EU moves towards addressing the above two challenges. The AI Act now provides further clarity on data training requirements , while the bloc’s investment into 19 AI factories is expected to significantly increase computing power (including access to supercomputers), data resources, and technical expertise.

The AI factories are also signaling a path towards a pan-European infrastructure, where open-source models have a big role to play. “We’re starting to see some cooperation and open-source is the best form of sharing,” Rijgersberg says.

“It’s about putting everything in the open to allow people to learn from each other, come up with new ideas, and collaborate to create new pan-European initiatives.”

The open-source approach offers benefits that go beyond the strategic imperative of digital sovereignty. It also ensures AI is accessible to everyone — from public institutions and major enterprises to small companies, universities, and individuals.

“The only way we are going to move our society forward in this space is by doing together," Rijgersberg remarks.

=== Building a board game's digital version with coding agents ===

By Cascha van Wanrooij

I’m an ML Engineer at KickstartAI. Over the summer, I wanted to spend a few spare hours on something useful and fun, so I volunteered to help with a digital version of the Zero Hunger Game . This physical board game for education is an initiative of the Zero Hunger Lab at Tilburg University, designed in collaboration with Breda University of Applied Sciences .

Essentially, the game teaches students about the second UN Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) by focusing on the unequal food distribution around the world. During gameplay, students try to feed their populations as best as they can.

What I appreciate about the game is that it exposes students to hard choices without telling them what to think, and even touches on questions around what it feels like to have to ask for food. I was a spectator during parts of the physical game’s design process and I once played it with early elementary school kids in a previous job. That helped a lot when I started sketching a digital prototype. I do not want to spoil mechanics here. If this interests you, you can read more about the Zero Hunger Lab’s project and consider playing the physical game when you can.

Mind you, I’m not a game designer, nor a frontend engineer. But I can read React and I’m comfortable with FastAPI. Since the physical board game already exists, the game’s guide effectively serves as a complete specification, every rule and possible player action is already defined and tested. That made the project a good experiment to push today’s AI coding agents as far as I could. I’m also an avid reader of Simon Willison’s blog. Let’s use his recent definition of an AI agent to anchor this article too: an LLM agent runs tools in a loop to achieve a goal.


>>> What we are building and why <<<

From what I understand, teachers in the Netherlands and elsewhere have shown interest in the Zero Hunger Game. Running the physical version takes a trained host who guides the rules and the flow. You also need the game box with the chips and cards to be at the school on the day, which puts a natural limit on where it can be played. A digital version can widen access, while also handling the rules. We still want students in the same physical room, since conversation works better face to face, which points to a format similar to Kahoot. A teacher opens a lobby on a host screen, puts it on the projector, and students join from laptops or phones with a code. The teacher advances rounds at the right moments while the software keeps state and nudges the flow.

We try to keep as much of the original look and feel as possible in the digital version. Some example screens:


>>> The technical choices <<<

I split the web design into three parts. First, a Python backend with FastAPI and a SQL database that tracks game state. Second, a host frontend for the teacher. And third, a player frontend for the students.

The frontends talk to the backend over WebSockets so everyone stays in sync as actions happen. I didn’t use a game engine because the web stack is what I know, it’s easy to deploy to browsers without installs, and coding agents are very fluent in this territory, thanks to a large amount of training data.

I began with Claude Code. My workflow was simple. Ask it to add a backend route for a new feature. Then have it write a small markdown note that documents the request and response for the frontends. Use that note as the spec when asking for the matching host and player UI. It worked because a FastAPI route doubles as both code and documentation.

Later, I tried GPT-5-Codex through the Codex CLI. It’s a fine-tuned GPT-5 specifically for coding. I found it slightly better for this project and cheaper per token, which mattered over many iterations.


>>> The AI-scout <<<

One pattern I picked up from a blog post by Josh Bleecher Snyder that helped a lot is to send out a scout. If you have played Age of Empires or StarCraft the analogy is obvious. Scouts are cheap units you send into the fog to learn something quickly. Here the scout is an agent you point at a fuzzy task with minimal constraints. You let it try, not because you expect a perfect solution, but because the attempt can reveal options or difficulties you hadn’t considered. Sometimes the scout gets lost. That’s fine. The point is what you learn for a few cents worth of tokens, not whether you land its exact edits.

To give a concrete example: the physical game ends each round with a short in-character announcement by the host that connects choices to consequences. I wanted a digital moment that felt thematic without turning into a wall of text. I sent a scout to explore end-of-round ideas. It proposed a newspaper front page that highlighted what happened, and it suggested tracking national wealth over rounds with a small chart. The chart was too complex for the target age, but the newspaper idea stuck.


>>> Silent collapse <<<

Andrej Karpathy talks about silent collapse in his conversation with Dwarkesh Patel , which clicked for me. You may be familiar with collapse in GANs . Individual samples look fine, yet together they cover only a narrow slice of what is possible. Karpathy calls this collapse in LLM’s “silent” because it’s not immediately obvious if you look at a single response. But try asking a model for a joke a 100 times, and you get variations of three of the same jokes. This lack of “entropy” limits the creative value that these models offer.

An example of not-so-silent collapse in a GAN model generating faces. The model has failed to capture the true variance of pictures of faces, and so all faces look alike, as you can see below:

In day to day work with coding agents, I feel a version of that in web design. Ask for a new screen and you get the same overused gradients and components. It looks polished in isolation but numbing as a set. This parody at vibe-coded.lol captures this blandness nicely. To my frustration, I encountered this many times while trying to expand the functionality of my frontend screens. Time and again, Codex would “drift” away from the current game design towards slick but cookie-cutter menus, clashing with current game design so far. This means that, for frontend design, I had to really step in and give Codex very small tasks with targeted instructions. Another way to try to fight this collapse was to sometimes switch to Claude Code. While the data these underlying models were trained on overlaps a lot, their tastes are not identical. This would sometimes nudge everything into a more creative direction.


>>> Concluding thoughts <<<

Building this has been humbling. I realized how much creativity went into crafting the original board game, and how even translating it to a browser already forces tough choices. When it comes to experimenting with AI agents, I’ll say it’s definitely been a success. Am I still the one injecting entropy when things start to feel too similar? Yes. But you can definitely use agents to boost creativity: send a cheap agent into the fog, see what it finds, keep only what helps, and do not worry if it gets lost. Finally, if you’re interested in the broader scope of things, Karpathy’s recent conversation with Dwarkesh is a good counterweight to the current agent hype. In fact, if you haven’t seen it yet, why are you reading this slop?

=== Community Spotlight: Merlijn van Breugel & Iman Hashemi ===


--- How Ditto makes medical language easy to understand with AI ---

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

Every month, we spotlight one member for our community . At KickstartAI, we believe that meaningful progress starts with individuals — builders, researchers, founders, and practitioners — who are applying AI in bold, practical ways. Our Community Spotlight series celebrates their work, their challenges, and the lessons they’re learning along the way.

This month, we’re featuring Merlijn van Breugel , co-founder and CPO at Ditto, and Iman Hashemi , Head of AI & Data. They're on a mission to bridge the healthcare communication gap.

Visits to doctors or hospitals often come with an added challenge: making sense of the medical information that follows. This is exactly what Merlijn van Breugel, Iman Hashemi, and the Ditto team have set out to change.

Founded in 2024, Ditto has developed a free app that uses AI to translate complex medical information into plain language. The aim is to help people better understand their health and diagnosis, regardless of medical condition, language differences, or age.

Users can record their doctor appointment to receive clear AI summaries, or scan medical documents to get clearer explanations. They can also share the information with family and friends. But how does it all work, especially in a field where accuracy and trust are paramount?


--- Under the hood ---

Ditto has opted not to build its own LLM from scratch, but instead add intelligence to foundation models.“We can go faster and differentiate more if we use the best model out there in the best possible way rather than spend all our resources on developing our own,” says Merlijn, the startup’s co-founder and CPO.

Big models are only one part of Ditto’s AI system, which also comprises Juvoly's Grutto model for speech recognition, a proprietary library for LLMOps, and various external tools for governance.

A core part of the system is explainability, adds Iman, Ditto’s Head of AI and Data. “For every AI summary, we need to know exactly what happens. What models we use, what versions we use, what decisions were made in the whole pipeline.”

The second key element is the ability to develop or add new technologies very quickly. “We've created this template where if you want to add something new, you can very easily just add it on top of basically one file, run it, and get it to production fast,” Iman says.


--- Evaluating the AI summaries ---

To assess the quality of its AI summaries, Ditto has developed a framework of metrics with input from external experts. This framework combines two complementary methods: human evaluation through a panel of doctors and patients as well as automated assessments.

Part of the automated analysis involves measuring how confident the model is in what it generates, based on the probabilities it assigns to each predicted word. This helps flag uncertainty in cases where the input is ambiguous — for instance, when a doctor mentions two different dosages.

Ditto also uses LLM judges to evaluate overall quality, such as completeness and understandability, while more traditional NLP methods run similarity searches to verify details. “For example, to cross-check medicine names for accuracy,” Iman says.


--- The hallucination challenge ---

Among all metrics, hallucinations are the hardest to manage, Merlijn says. “They’re also the most serious aspect, and this is where most of our attention goes to.”

One significant risk factor is incorrect medication details. “ You can have a perfect summary but the wrong medicine name, and that can be very harmful,” he explains. For this reason, if Ditto’s system detects inconsistency in drug names or dosages, the app doesn’t generate a summary. “It’s annoying, but safer. I’d rather share no information than information I know is wrong.”

But not all hallucinations are clear-cut. Some are harmless, like when the model adds generic advice such as “call your doctor if symptoms get worse.” “Some doctors like that,” Merlijn says, “others don’t, because they didn’t explicitly say it.”

“This is why we have multiple guardrails in place and a severity classification of hallucinations,” Iman adds. “We only want to share summaries that are safe and we prefer being on the conservative side.”


--- Building trust ---

Building trust into an AI product for healthcare is no easy task, but as Merlijn explains, Ditto benefits from being closely connected to the Dutch healthcare system .

Through both professional and personal ties, the team collaborates with different organizations and academic hospitals not only to present its solution, but also to engage in open dialogue about how to build it responsibly.

“Most of the doctors are very positive; others are more skeptical and have expressed privacy concerns about the recording feature,” Merlijn says. “What’s been working well for us in encouraging clinicians to test our app themselves. That has convinced many to start suggesting Ditto to their patients!”

Gaining user trust has to do a lot with clarity, he adds. “We try to be very clear on how to use the app, communicate why certain things wouldn’t work, and be transparent about privacy and data protection. We have consciously decided to not store medical data centrally, such that it is only stored on-device. Our users love that.”


--- Launching an AI product in less than a year ---

The startup launched its app in July this year, approximately seven months after the team started working on the product idea.

“It all starts with having the best people,” Merlijn says, adding the importance of creating “maker time” for the development team. “This means having the ability to make quick decisions instead of involving multiple people in endless discussions — and trusting each other to make the right call.”

Executing at speed is also about scoping things down and embracing ambiguity.

“In the beginning, I struggled with that because I was used to roles with extensive  structure and plans. But being comfortable with ambiguity allows you to be more agile: if you suddenly have a better idea, you can directly change course and move with that idea.”

Ditto has set big ambitions for the future. “There’s plenty of room to grow in the Netherlands next year, and I’m quite certain that there will eventually be international expansion,” Merlijn says. Product-wise, the startup will also focus on summary personalization and on extending the functionality of sharing with loved ones.

“This first year has definitely been a rollercoaster, with some hard lessons along the way,” he concludes. “But building a startup is the most fun I’ve ever had. Above all, working on something meaningful, something that helps people in such a tangible way, is what truly drives the team."

=== The drawbacks of benchmark-driven AI research ===

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

When the ImageNet paper was published in 2009, it set a defining ML benchmark , shaping the course of modern AI research and development – from introducing a data-centric approach to paving the way for deep learning’s dominance.

“At the time, this benchmark played a great role,” says Maarten Stol, Chief Scientific Officer at Braincreators. “Having ImageNet’s dataset available triggered a surge in new research for novel methods.”

“But the first drawback with benchmarks,” he adds, “is that eventually we all score very high. As a result, they lose their differentiating power.”

Maarten also points to a related concern: the familiar graphs often shown in AI talks, where model accuracy rises steadily and gradually approaches the horizontal “human performance” line.

Consider, for instance, an image-classification task such as detecting cancer in X-ray scans. “Let's say human performance is at 90%, while a machine learning model performs higher, at 92%,” he says.

Looking at the numbers alone, the conclusion is that the model is better at detecting cancer than a human doctor.

But this, according to Maarten, is misleading. He argues that the most revealing evaluation of performance isn’t what a model or a human did right. It’s what they did wrong.

If a human doctor, for example, misclassifies 10% of the cases, the most likely reason behind this misclassification would be that the examples are too ambiguous or extremely difficult. When a model, however, fails in 8% of the cases, some of them would also be challenging – but others would be obvious mistakes that a trained professional would rarely, if ever, make.

“The fact that the model scores better on the frequency of a benchmark doesn’t mean that it has visual understanding, or that it understands what it’s doing,” Maarten notes. “It only means that it scores well on that particular benchmark.”

“If we combine this with the problem of differentiation, we’re eventually running out of interesting error cases. These are the cases that show us how a model makes mistakes and what we should improve.”

“I think that the sometimes unhealthy benchmarking culture, the hunting for incremental results, only focuses on how much you're doing right. But it's not looking at the quality and the nature of the mistakes that you're making.”


>>> The quest for meaning <<<

But then the question is: if we move from quantity into quality, how will we be able to evaluate it? After all, quality is about interpretation, which is inherently linked to subjectivity – a non-fitting element for science.

“We’d need another leap like ImageNet,” Stol says. “One that would come not only with millions of images, but also with hundreds of thousands of  interpretations and a specific, statistical distribution of these text descriptions in the latent space, in their vector database, and so on.”

This direction could be plausible, he adds, but it would require significant research focus. From his side, Stol is researching what he calls “notions of hardness:” what makes a problem or instance hard.

Stol comes back to the X-ray example. What he’s looking for is an explanation of what makes it hard for a machine learning model to accurately identify the scans.

“I think we need to push machine learning research into this sort of area where we start to understand these error cases we’re left with, that 8% that we never solve,” he says.

“What do these error cases have in common? Can we start to group them? Can we start to generate them? Can we start to control them? Because if we can have controlled notions of hardness, we can actually 'benchmark' on meaning instead of on frequency, accuracy, and scores.”

Stol links this to the idea of “model provenance” as proposed in “ A Hierarchy of Limitations in Machine Learning .” In that work, modeling is understood as a series of choices that narrow what a system can capture, locking in certain meanings while setting others aside. Once you commit to a quantitative framing, Stol explains, forms of qualitative human interpretation and context fall outside of the scope unless you deliberately bring them back in.

Although the paper is a preprint, it does provide an interesting thought experiment.

“What if, at some point, we could manage this provenance? What if we could track this hierarchy of limitations and say: we have a quantitative model. It’s a black box neural network. It performs well at a predefined task, but around it we’ve also collected a number of videos, audio, and any other qualitative input that has meaning.”

“And maybe there’s a way to reinterpret the quantitative model on the fly, – or in a more science-fiction scenario – even integrate that meaning directly.”

“This is a future dream,” Stol says, adding that he doesn't have the answers. But he thinks it’s important to have a clear vision towards this type of future.

“If we care about ethics and safety, our AI models must be about meaning. They must be about people.”

=== Exploring multimodal AI models for biodiversity ===

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

Biodiversity is under mounting pressure as a result of climate change and human activity, with species and ecosystems shifting faster than our ability to monitor and understand them. Conservation efforts increasingly depend on new tools to track these changes, identify emerging risks, and inform where interventions are most needed

Against this backdrop, AI is becoming an important part of biodiversity research. Generative AI in particular, together with a new generation of multimodal models, can combine different types of data into unified systems for biodiversity analysis, monitoring, and prediction. Some aim to forecast biodiversity change across entire regions; others focus on extracting patterns from visual data to track individual animals.

Two examples of how this is taking shape in practice can be seen in the work of Thanasis Trantas , AI Research Scientist at TNO , and Rita Pucci , Assistant Professor at the Leiden Institute for Advanced Computer Science ( LIACS ) at Leiden University.


--- A foundation model for biodiversity ---

According to its makers, BioAnalyst is the world’s first large-scale, multimodal foundation model specifically designed for biodiversity. “Most existing foundation models for ecology are focusing on very concrete use cases, usually at the classification layer, but we found none that works as a spatio-temporal forecaster or predictor,” says Thanasis Trantas, AI Research Scientist at TNO.

This was one of the main reasons the team decided to build its own, which they released in June 2025. Their main source of inspiration was Microsoft’s Aurora , a large-scale foundation model for weather and climate forecasting.

BioAnalyst is tailored to biodiversity analysis and conservation planning. It combines 10 different modalities, such as climate, vegetation, and species distribution. It can be fine-tuned for a series of downstream tasks, including habitat suitability assessments and population trend forecasting.

“A key differentiation of our architecture is that we don’t rely on frozen backbones or foundation models to generate representations for each modality,” Trantas explains. “By using Perceiver for both encoding and decoding, we instead take all modalities in their raw form and fuse them directly in the encoder. The idea is to do as little pre-processing as possible, just normalization, and let the model make sense of the raw information itself.”

With this unified setup, the model can capture interactions among modalities at different data scales and distinguish features across domains such as ground conditions, atmospheric processes, and species distributions.


>>> Training challenges <<<

The lack of training data was the first challenge the team had to address. “When we started, we came into a wall,” Trantas says. “There was no data that could facilitate the pre-training or fine-tuning of the model. We then decided to create our own dataset.”

The team built BioCube , which compiles multiple datasets (from atmospheric and climate to edaphic and species distribution variables), all grounded to the same spatiotemporal system. It spans 20 years, with data from 2000 to 2020.

Another major challenge was access to computing power for training and development. The team managed to secure access for one year and a half to Snellius, the supercomputer located at SURF, via an NWO grant.

Trantas explains that the compute resources available to them – in terms of hours of use, consultancy, and storage capacity – shaped significantly the model’s development.

One example is BioAnalyst’s resolution. “Imagine that for 20 years of data, looking at a temporal dimension of 6-hours and 0.25 degrees (~27km) of spatial grid sampling for multiple data modalities, the storage requirements can reach up to more than 1,000 terabytes,” he says. “We chose a different approach and upsampled our model to support a monthly resolution, as in any case there aren’t too many biodiversity observations that change day to day.”

Trantas adds that they needed to build two different architectures: a big one to actually pre-train the model, and a smaller one to use for debugging and to run experiments faster. “We also had to have a distributed training framework, so that the model could be distributed in multiple GPUs and compute modes, to be trained faster.”


>>> Creating a baseline for future models <<<

With no existing benchmarks, the team evaluated BioAnalyst using standard metrics, ecological measures, and comparisons with baseline machine learning models and Aurora, showing the model generalizes well across diverse biodiversity prediction tasks.

Current limitations include BioAnalyst’s specific focus on European land-based biodiversity as well as the model’s deterministic nature and the challenge of uncertainty quantification of its predictions – all of which can be part of future work.

“The innovation here isn't the model itself. It's the engineering of the whole system that can produce an adequate prediction,” Trantas says. “We don’t have the perfect prediction results yet – and there aren’t any to begin with.”

“What we want to be is the baseline and we invite people to start building on top of what we created.” This is why the team has open-sourced all parts of its work, including their preprint , the model codebase , the fine-tuning techniques , the model weights , and the dataset creation codebase .


--- Bio-inspired computer vision for population monitoring ---

Identifying individual animals in the wild is central to biodiversity monitoring. For species like tigers, zebras, leopards, and zebrafish, unique skin patterns, such as stripes and spots, offer a natural biometric signature. Yet teaching AI systems to reliably recognize individuals from these patterns is no easy task.

“Individual identification through skin pattern formation is a key method for population monitoring,” says Rita Pucci, Assistant Professor at LIACS. “But automating it is challenging. In images of animals in nature, you have to tell the model that the background doesn’t matter, the species doesn’t matter – only the skin pattern matters.”

“During my post-doc, I tried traditional methods, like convolutional neural networks, but they didn’t work. The model was focusing on the background. Cutting out the animal didn’t help either.”

Pucci is now following a different approach in a PhD project she’s supervising, titled “TATTOOs: Pattern formation through bio-inspired computer vision,” with PhD student Jens van Bijsterveld.

“We need to make the model focus on patterns alone to identify individuals, which means that it needs the knowledge necessary to understand these patterns,” she says.

To do that, the project focuses on building a machine learning model, based on computer vision, that integrates the mechanisms of pattern formation directly into its learning process. In practice, this means incorporating a mechanistic model – specifically the Turing reaction–diffusion model – to explain to the ML system how patterns form.


>>> Architecture and training <<<

Pucci describes the model as an autoencoder architecture consisting of two modules: an encoder which focuses on extracting features from the pattern in the input image (close to representational learning); and a decoder which uses these features to learn how to reconstruct the visual representation of the pattern as in the input image. These processes happen simultaneously during training, allowing the model to develop knowledge about the different skin patterns.

After training, the decoder is removed. “That’s because we’re not interested in generating images for individuals,” Pucci says. The encoding part that remains is the most important one for the project: it retains all the necessary pixel information to represent and regenerate the pattern.

These encoded features are then used as classifiers. The model learns to cluster the slightly different representations for the same individual animal under a single label.

The team is currently applying this method to zebrafish, a species for which extensive data is available. Large video datasets allow them to track individuals and assemble thousands of labeled images per fish, a scale necessary for developing and validating a pattern-focused representation.


>>> Key challenges <<<

When asked about the technical challenges involved in developing this model, Pucci begins with the training images. “We’re studying fish and underwater images aren’t ideal, even in the best possible setup of a fish tank,” she says. Meanwhile, all data comes from video frames, which introduces its own complications. A significant issue is the movement of the fish, changing the angle and shape of their body. As a result, the visible pattern also shifts slightly in every frame.

Still, the team chose zebrafish for a practical reason: compared to other maculate animals, they make it possible to build much larger datasets. “It’s much easier to collect enough images of 200 zebrafish individuals than, say, 200 tigers,” Pucci notes.

The integration of a mechanistic model inside or outside of the main ML model is another big challenge, as the two perform "completely different” functions. On the one hand, the mechanistic model is rule-based and time-dependent; on the other hand, the ML model is an adaptive system that learns through optimization. “The combination of different types of data – in this case, images and information from the mechanistic model – is also an open challenge in multimodal models like ours,” Pucci adds.

“These are big, but not impossible challenges,” she says. The team is currently at the early stages of research, running ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of the mechanistic model. If the project proves successful, the model can be used for population monitoring combined with the detection of related issues, such as inbreeding.

Pucci herself is particularly interested in various types of multimodal models, such as those that can fuse heterogeneous types of data like images, audio, and DNA sequences. She adds that interest in multimodal models for biodiversity is gradually increasing. “We now have enormous amounts of data we can use to train these models and we also have state-of-the-art technology already tested in medicine.”

=== A conversation with Erick Webbe, KickstartAI's new CEO ===


--- 'The Netherlands must move from talking about AI to doing AI' ---

By Rutger Huizenga

Erick Webbe loves places where things are made: spaces with bright light where people use their hands, head, and heart to build something new. Perhaps that’s why he immediately felt at home at KickstartAI, the independent foundation aiming to help the Netherlands finally move from AI ambitions to AI applications.

As of January 1 2026, he is the new CEO. And although he’s only 41, he brings a career that seems almost tailor-made for this role: trained as a physicist, starting his career in strategy consulting, growing into a data specialist, and eventually responsible for the AI organization at Bol. On top of that, he has been on the board of KickstartAI for several years on behalf of Ahold Delhaize.

“Curiosity is my engine,” he says. “I always want to understand how things work: why the sky is blue, why a rainbow curves… those were the questions that kept me busy as a kid. It also explains why I played chess while my friends played football. Chess is actually a perfect metaphor for AI: everyone has the same pieces and the same information, but the difference lies in how you use them. It’s exactly the same with AI.”


>>> From Bol to the National AI Challenge <<<

Webbe spent over ten years at Bol, where he saw how AI can truly transform an organization. Experiments remain useful for exploration, he says, but real impact comes from scaling. “At Bol, dozens of AI applications are now running daily: from delivery forecasts and personal recommendations to logistics optimization and fraud prevention. This transformation has significantly increased operational efficiency and improved the customer experience.”

The experience also sparked a broader idea: if it works there, why not across the whole country? “It’s an ambitious goal,” he acknowledges. “We have leading universities and a rich technological tradition – Wifi, Bluetooth, and Python were all developed or advanced with Dutch contribution – and we have strong companies. Yet globally we perform quite poorly when it comes to adoption. That’s because we focus too much on risks and too little on value. While AI, used wisely, can actually help solve major societal challenges: labor shortages, pressure on healthcare, pressure on cities.”

To illustrate this, he likes to use a homey metaphor: “My daughters, seven and nine, are learning to cut vegetables. First with a protective cover, then with a blunt knife. But at some point they also need to learn to handle a sharp knife. You don’t hide knives because they’re dangerous; you teach children how to use them responsibly. With AI, we must do exactly the same.”


>>> KickstartAI: from talking to doing <<<

That pragmatic perspective is exactly what attracts him to KickstartAI. The foundation, created by KLM, NS, Ahold Delhaize and ING, deliberately focuses on applied AI: projects that don’t live in a lab but in real organizations, with real users. That translation is clearly visible in the projects from recent years. Webbe lists a few, visibly enthusiastic:

“With KLM, for example, we plan more precisely how many meals are needed on board, reducing waste. For NS, we show that abandoned luggage at stations can be detected without tracking individual travelers. At Bol, we predict more accurately how many packages will be processed each day, so delivery vans enter cities less often while half-empty. And at ING, we’re building a multilingual chatbot for financial health that helps people with financial questions in clear, understandable language.”

And all of this with only twenty employees. Webbe smiles. “People often underestimate what a small team can achieve when you work smart and have strong partners. ING has about eight million customers in the Netherlands and is part of people’s financial routines every single day. Ahold Delhaize, with Albert Heijn, fills millions of plates every day. NS transports more than a million travelers daily and literally keeps the country moving. And KLM connects the Netherlands with the rest of the world through a network a small organization could never build alone. Through these partners we reach almost everyone in the Netherlands, sometimes multiple times a year.”

=== Fuel planning optimization with machine learning: the KLM challenge ===

By Jeroen Nelen

In aviation, fuel planning is an essential but complex task that requires optimizing efficiency while ensuring the aircraft carries the required amount of fuel. At KickstartAI, we believe that AI holds great potential to improve this process, and we put it to the test in our recent project with KLM. This project, called Daidalos, aims to optimize contingency fuel: the reserve fuel planes carry for unforeseen circumstances, such as delays, rerouting, and adverse weather.

Part of this project is a challenge, a two-week structured hackathon organized by KickstartAI and KLM to improve the baseline of the initial proof of concept (PoC). During the challenge, the participants looked at new data sources, different models, and explainability techniques. This blog dives into the problem that sparked the project and details the challenge setup and its preliminary results.


>>> The problem <<<

As mentioned above, contingency fuel is the reserve fuel planes carry to handle unforeseen events during the flight. Every airline is required to carry contingency fuel, but calculating the exact amount varies between different companies.

KLM, in particular, has a statistical approach that is generally performing well. However, one limitation of traditional statistical models is their challenge in anticipating rare outliers which can be particularly significant when it comes to contingency fuel planning. Recognizing this, KLM saw an opportunity to investigate ML including the incorporation of additional data sources and features, to predict a flight-specific amount of contingency fuel required.

By harnessing these innovations, the goal is to better tailor contingency fuel loads, adding more when unusual conditions are detected, and safely optimizing the amount carried when possible. Overestimating the amount of contingency fuel adds more weight on board, increasing the overall fuel consumption, a concept known as “cost-to-carry.”

For two weeks, 12 data scientists from Ahold Delhaize, ING , KLM, and KickstartAI worked on the problem at hand. We grouped the participants into three teams, each asked to build a complete machine learning solution.

We  broke the ML pipeline down into three work packages: validating new data sources, testing different ML models, and exploring explainability techniques. Each team member had to focus on one of these components and work together with other teams to share knowledge. To make sure the hackathon was about improving the baseline, KLM together with KickstartAI provided an evaluation script that automatically calculated the two important axes and key performance indicators (KPIs); cost-to-carry excess fuel and contingency efficiency.


>>> The work packages: <<<

The challenge focused on three objectives, which were:

Validating new data sources: Participants were given unexplored data to perform additional analysis and develop features. The goal was to identify new features that had a positive effect on the performance of the model. The data sources included weather data, airport-specific information, and historical flight data.

The goal was to identify new features that had a positive effect on the performance of the model. The data sources included weather data, airport-specific information, and historical flight data.

>>> Results: <<<

WP1: Data analysis & features

One of the goals of the challenge was to investigate unexplored data sources.  The first source was TAF forecasted weather data and the second source was METAR actual weather data. We provided this data because weather is one of the main reasons why flights divert from their route. After the raw data was parsed, it was possible to transform it into features, such as wind direction, rainfall, or visibility on the departure and arrival airport. Surprisingly, these features did not improve the model significantly during the challenge.

The second data source consisted of historical records of all scheduled international flights of the last two years. We used this data to estimate how busy an airport is at a specific moment and if there might be congestion at the arriving airport. Based on an inferred capacity, we could calculate the likelihood of congestion by comparing the expected number of movements against different capacity proxies. The different proxies that were tried during the challenge were: the total number of runways of an airport, the historical hourly maximum movements on that specific hour of the day on that airport, and lastly, how the current traffic deviates from a dynamic baseline. This baseline was calculated by taking a 4-week rolling average of that specific airport, day of the week, and hour.

The third feature we added was a set of lagged features, feeding the model with additional data points from the previous 7 days. The rationale was that recent events could indicate underlying causes for delays or deviations in trip fuel consumption. This lagged feature had a positive influence on the performance of the model.

Lastly, we provided a lot of features extracted from the Operational Flight Plan (OFP). This included distance, flight time, airplane details, and more. A technique called Reverse Feature Ablation was used to filter out all features that did not have a significant effect on the output. This final set of features made it possible to beat the baseline.

WP2: Modeling

For the baseline model we used LightGBM, which proved to be the best performing model during the challenge as well. Nevertheless, we were able to make two notable improvements that boosted LightGBM final performance.

The first one was removing extreme outliers from the training dataset, which resulted in better performance on the KPIs. This filter was not applied on the test set, as these outliers are an important part of the evaluation. Secondly, we post-processed the predictions by adding a safety margin to each prediction. This simple step made the model significantly more reliable, while not increasing the amount of unused fuel. We then used hyperparameter tuning to find the optimal balance between these two parameters.

It must be noted here that while these solutions contributed to the final KPIs, they didn’t increase the model performance when looking at the evaluation metrics such as the pinball loss . This might be solved with a custom loss function, but this isn’t something we tried during the challenge.

WP3: Explainable AI

The last workpackage focused on explaining the output of the model to the pilots and the dispatchers. To achieve this, we explored multiple explainability techniques and visualizations. The final prototype was built using Streamlit and included a couple of interesting features.

The first technique was local feature importances using SHAP values. This technique can be used to show which features were contributing the most to the output and how. Pilots could use this information to further understand whether the contingency fuel was sufficient for the circumstances of that flight. On top of this logic, we built another feature we called “what-if.” This tries to answer the question: “ If I change feature X, what happens to the final output?”.

Lastly, we included additional information about the current flight and the previous flights from that same route. Together, these explainability features and visuals can provide pilots with a clearer view into model decisions, and therefore encourage adoption of the ML solution.


>>> Conclusion and next steps <<<

During the KLM & KickstartAI challenge, 12 participants took a fresh look at the problem and built an improved solution. They turned new data sources into interesting features, tested different models, and took the first step toward explaining the model’s results.

In the next phase, the team will validate and further discuss the solution with the business stakeholders, before moving to implementation. Once the ML model is approved, KLM will compare it in practice with the current solution and continue testing. The pilots will also receive the model’s output along with clear explanations — an important step toward full adoption of this machine learning model.

--- Dr Pauldy Otermans & Dev Aditya on building offline AI teachers ---

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

Every month, we spotlight one member for our community . At KickstartAI, we believe that meaningful progress starts with individuals – builders, researchers, founders, and practitioners – who are applying AI in bold, practical ways. Our Community Spotlight series celebrates their work, their challenges, and the lessons they’re learning along the way.

This month, we’re featuring Dr Pauldy Otermans, co-founder and Principal & Head of AI Research at Otermans Institute, and Dev Aditya, co-founder and Managing Director/CEO & AI Lead. This is their story.

Dr Pauldy Otermans and Dev Aditya met at university in London. Sharing a passion for education, they traveled to South Asia to teach students in marginalized communities – and experienced first-hand the lack of sufficient educational support.

Their early work quickly led to the first government MOU from the state of Uttarakhand in India to teach and train students soft and employability skills. Soon after, the duo founded Otermans Institute with the mission to make learning accessible to all and upskill 750 million learners by 2030.


>>> From fieldwork to scaling <<<

When the pandemic hit two months later, Pauldy and Dev moved their learning programs online, teaching via Zoom with the support of teachers, trainers, and volunteers. “At some point in 2020, we reached 35,000 learners,” Dev says. “Since our focus was on actual teaching rather than simply providing content, we realized we needed a different approach to be able to scale.”

The team decided to experiment with AI around the same time that the first Transformer architecture papers were emerging, hinting at the new possibilities of the technology. In January 2021, they launched what they describe as the world’s first AI teacher – 18 months before the launch of ChatGPT.

“For the first lesson we created, we didn’t have enough training data,” Dev recalls. “So we wrote about 12,500 lines of questions and answers as a data layer.” Their first AI teacher, called Beatrice, was first used at BCF UNHCR refugee camps in Northern Iraq.

Fast-forward to today, Otermans Institute has expanded into 19 countries, reaching learners across the entire education spectrum – from primary and secondary school to higher education, skills development, vocational training, and national workforce programmes. “We’re content-agnostic,” Pauldy notes. “Our systems can teach almost anything.”

Some of their most significant recent work includes running Africa’s largest AI literacy programme, and a new agreement with the government of Burundi, where their AI teachers will support one million students from elementary school through high school.


>>> Behind the Otermans Institute's AI teachers <<<

Dubbed OIAI, the company’s conversational AI teachers are built on a combination of proprietary large and small language models. They follow Bloom’s taxonomy of learning , guiding learners through progressively deeper levels of understanding.

The system supports personalized learning and adapts to individual learning needs, including special education needs. “For those with learning difficulties such as dyslexia and ADD, autism, we’ve built in layers of accessibility,” Dev explains. This includes not only adjustments to the language the AI uses, but also a series of neurodiversity-friendly features – from background color and font size to subtitle styling, avatar type, and speaking speed.

While all AI teachers share the same architecture, their appearance and language changes depending on the country they’ll be used. “It could be the way they look, the accessories they have, and also the way they speak,” Pauldy explains.

But AI is only one part of the company’s technology stack, which also incorporates infrastructure, software as well as the end-consumer and data analytics layer.

In practice, the system begins with a dashboard where institutions and governments upload their curricula and course materials. The platform converts this content into structured lessons, which educators can then adjust or refine. Once approved, these lessons can reach learners through the AI teacher app.

“When we first started our average learner completion rate was 64%, which rose to 95% as of January 2025, including our free courses,” Dev adds.


>>> Going offline <<<

This year, the team made its biggest breakthrough yet: taking its AI teachers offline. “For marginalized communities the real bottleneck is connectivity,” Dev says. “Even when internet access exists, it often comes at a cost people can’t afford.”

By early autumn, the team had also reduced the offline app size from 1.2GB to 500MB.

In this setup, the phone effectively becomes the server, with the AI running directly on-device. That’s enabled by a mix of techniques: switching between LLMs and SLMs depending on the use case, and compressing the models through quantization.

But the decisive element, Dev says, is engineering. Rather than sending an entire user interaction to the model at once, the system breaks it into small, sequential steps – making the workload manageable even for a lightweight model. And behind this sits a runtime that switches components on and off as needed, ensuring the device’s limited memory is used efficiently.

“Our architecture is hybrid,” he explains. “90% of activity runs offline on the edge, directly on the user’s device, while 10% runs on the cloud.”


>>> Ethics in AI teaching <<<

This hybrid architecture plays a key role in the company’s ethical framework, the two founders explain. Because the system runs offline, learners’ personal data stays on their device rather than being sent to remote servers, reducing risks typically associated with cloud-based AI.

At the same time, the small part of the system that runs on the cloud and accesses the internet collects anonymous, aggregated data, which it feeds back to partner organizations. These analytics help institutions identify learning gaps and assess the effectiveness of their materials, informing decisions about educational content and policy.

This is particularly important for countries like Burundi, Uganda, and rural Kenya,” Dev says, “where we’re helping them capture learning data that was previously recorded only on paper.”

“Our aim is to make education accessible to everyone,” Pauldy concludes. “Our AI teachers are not here to take anyone’s job.They can be the first point of learning for people who would otherwise have no access to education, and they can support learners outside classroom hours when a human teacher simply isn’t available.”

=== How Earth observation and AI are opening up space data ===

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

For decades, Earth observation was largely removed from everyday decision-making. Satellite imagery and space data were mainly used by governments, research institutes, and specialised agencies, with limited reach beyond expert communities.

Today, that is changing. Satellite-derived insights are increasingly used in practical contexts, from agriculture and infrastructure planning to air-quality modelling and urban management – often by people with no background in space or geospatial analysis.

This shift reflects the broader evolution of the new space economy, where space data is becoming accessible to a wider range of users and applications. To explore how this transition is playing out in practice, we spoke with:

Parya Pasha Zadeh , Program Manager at NL Space Campus , Project Officer EO Science at the University of Twente , and Director and co-founder of Marmoris .

Parya Pasha Zadeh , Program Manager at NL Space Campus , Project Officer EO Science at the University of Twente , and Director and co-founder of Marmoris .

Thijs Oosterhuis, Solutions Architect for Geo-Information and Remote Sensing at Provincie Zuid-Holland.

Thijs Oosterhuis, Solutions Architect for Geo-Information and Remote Sensing at Provincie Zuid-Holland.


--- Opening Earth observation: data, access, and AI ---

The shift towards wider access to space data and Earth observation (EO) applications results from three main changes over the past decade, according to Pasha Zadeh.

The first one is the availability of public space data. Especially under Copernicus , the EU’s Earth observation programme, Sentinel satellites operated by the European Space Agency began providing systematic satellite data from the mid-2010s onwards, making large volumes of EO imagery openly and reliably available.

The second change is the rise of commercial satellite operators – alongside the boom of satellite constellation deployment – that not only provide frequent captures of EO data, but also offer higher resolution images.


>>> From automation to insight <<<

The third key shift is AI, Pasha Zadeh adds. While machine learning began to be adopted at scale in Earth observation in the early 2000s, recent advances in AI are significantly expanding the range of possibilities, such as enabling more data-driven insights.

“Take spatial multicriteria analysis for site selection as part of a conservation project,” she says. “Traditionally, you would use different factors (like time series EO data and end user perspective), combine them as criteria in a GIS decision-support tool, and come up with a site-selection recommendation.”

“What we are now exploring is using AI to learn through the different layers of data which factors are more important, and weigh those criteria to make a decision.”

In a spatial planning context, this could mean combining different data sources over time – like air quality, land use, demographics, or income – to reveal trends such as increasing housing demand that can help explore where to build new houses, adds Thijs Oosterhuis, Solutions Architect Geo at Provincie Zuid-Holland.

“By adding AI to the mix, you can potentially explore scenarios from the opposite perspective. For example, how building new housing or an industrial area in a certain location might affect water quality, land use, or socio-economic patterns over time. The possibilities of modelling our surroundings can become endless,” he emphasizes.

Another, already visible, benefit of modern AI is the higher speed of satellite data processing. Satellite imagery is extremely data-heavy, with each pixel containing tens or even hundreds of measured values, and much of it obscured by cloud cover.

“Earlier, we used to receive all the data and did the pre-processing on the ground – cloud masking, removing water bodies, and then analysing only the usable parts,” Pasha Zadeh  explains. “Now, newer satellites use on-board AI and edge computing to pre-process the data and send only what’s relevant, which saves a significant amount of storage and bandwidth.”


>>> Bridging gaps and lowering barriers <<<

AI could also help bridge gaps in Earth observation data, particularly when satellite imagery is captured at different spatial and temporal resolutions, adds Oosterhuis.

“Sentinel data, for example, is available at around 10-metre resolution with regular coverage every few days,” he explains. “Data from commercial satellites can be available at three metres with near-daily coverage, or at sub-metre resolution but only a few times a year and at less predictable moments. That means you don’t always have the same level of detail at every point in time.”

“Helping bridge some of the temporal gaps could be a promising added function for AI,” he says.

Both Pasha Zadeh and Oosterhuis see emerging opportunities in using LLMs to allow end users to interact with space data directly. “If we can make a common user interact with spatial data in a conversational way, we're opening more doors for the uptake of this type of information,” Pasha Zadeh explains.


--- Earth observation markets and applications ---

The Earth observation market has traditionally been dominated by public-sector users, but this is gradually changing. While governments and public institutions remain the largest consumers of EO data, a broader set of application domains and emerging markets is now taking shape, according to both Pasha Zadeh and Oosterhuis.

One of the most established areas for EO is, naturally, climate monitoring and prediction, as Pasha Zadeh explains, where the focus is less on fine spatial detail and more on consistent, long-term measurements. Closely linked is also environmental monitoring, including biodiversity and ecosystem services.

“There are several AI-powered EO products that can be used for detecting deforestation, monitoring land-use and land-cover change, identifying habitat loss, and detecting pollution,” she says. Alongside these uses, EO also underpins long-standing application areas such as disaster risk management and food security.


>>> Novel applications in environmental impact assessment <<<

Pasha Zadeh’s startup, Marmoris , specializes in environmental impact assessments for coastal and aquatic habitats. As part of a recent project, Pasha Zadeh is exploring a new EO application: using satellite data to have an indication of biodiversity in water – for inland water bodies, the coast, or ports.

“We don’t know if this is possible and we’re currently in the research phase,” she says. The approach combines satellite-based observations with eDNA samples, water quality indicators, and different environmental parameters.

If successful, Pasha Zadeh argues, this could save time and money, enable biodiversity monitoring to scale, and provide early warnings of threats. Potential use cases include conservation and nature-based projects, as well as identifying damage from activities such as construction or tourism.


>>> EO in policy and regional governance <<<

Oosterhuis’ work illustrates how EO is used in practice at the regional policy level. As a solutions architect for geo-information and remote sensing at Provincie Zuid-Holland, his focus is on bringing together many different datasets. “The main goal is to combine a lot of different data sets,” he explains. “That can be satellite data, but also data measured on the ground, policy data, airborne data, or other geographic data.”

He describes this as assembling different puzzle pieces to form a more complete picture that can support policy makers with information ranging from vegetation monitoring dune areas to tracking degradation of road markings over time.

At the core of this approach is a lakehouse, where all these data sources are brought together before more advanced analysis or AI is applied. To structure all the data, the team is also building a data cube , which overlays a hexagonal grid across South Holland. Essentially, each hexagon has a unique index and contains multiple data layers for a specific location. These layers are stored in the cube with a time dimension, allowing multi-year comparisons and trend analysis.

“It’s about using the right pieces together to see what’s actually happening and to identify what kind of extra pieces might be needed,” Oosterhuis says.

More broadly, this represents a shift towards more data-driven policymaking, Oosterhuis argues. Instead of relying on point-based measurements or static reports, policy makers can work with continuously updated data across space and time, allowing them to better understand patterns, trade-offs, and long-term effects of their decisions.


>>> Emerging markets and commercial applications <<<

Both Pasha Zadeh and Oosterhuis see signs of growing commercial interest. Pasha Zadeh points to ESG compliance, sequestration monitoring, and mining as areas where EO is increasingly relevant, bringing more private companies into the market.

“We’ve seen several acquisitions of Earth observation companies by larger investors, as well as increasing investment in EO startups from outside the space sector,” she says. ““That’s a strong indication of the growth potential in the space data and applications market.”

Oosterhuis highlights agriculture and commodities as another example, where satellite data can be used to monitor crop growth and estimate yields at scale, feeding into market analysis. He also points to longer-term possibilities such as biodiversity credits – instead of carbon credits – measured with satellite data, as a future direction for more market-driven EO applications.


--- How does the Netherlands fare in Earth observation? ---

Asked about the Netherlands’ position in Earth observation, both Pasha Zadeh and Oosterhuis point to a strong ecosystem.

“For the size of the country, we have quite a lot of companies working in EO, and some of them have been around for a long time.” While the Netherlands is often associated with space hardware because ESA’s ESTEC is based there, she stresses that the country is also strong on the data side, particularly in terms of EO uptake.

That uptake is particularly visible across public institutions, from national ministries to provinces and municipalities, which have long been among the main users of geodata and Earth observation.

Oosterhuis also sees the Netherlands as having a strong position in Earth observation, but frames it less as a centre of innovation and more as a connector. “The Netherlands is very good as a kind of broker,” he says, “a reseller of innovative ideas rather than a breeding ground of innovation in itself.”

This is why he stresses the need for continuous investment. For Oosterhuis collaboration is also central. “Ideally, it’s a triangle,” he says, referring to government, companies, and universities. “The government brings practical use cases, companies bring technology, and universities bring knowledge about new opportunities.”

=== Community Spotlight ===


--- Stan Dumont on how agentic AI can bridge the data-business gap ---

By Ioanna Lykiardopoulou

Every month, we spotlight one member for our community . At KickstartAI, we believe that meaningful progress starts with individuals – builders, researchers, founders, and practitioners – who are applying AI in bold, practical ways. Our Community Spotlight series celebrates their work, their challenges, and the lessons they’re learning along the way.

This month, we’re featuring Stan Dumont , Data Scientist at Lynxx . With a background in econometrics, Stan now focuses on deploying ML and GenAI agents particularly for the transport sector.

For Stan Dumont, data scientist at Lynxx, a big challenge for AI professionals is to bridge the data gap between data and business. That gap is exactly where he believes agentic AI can help: shifting us from an era of seeing data to an era of talking with it.

“As a community, we’re very good at creating clear overviews, polished dashboards, and compelling visualizations,” Stan says. But every new insight those tools surface, he adds, also generates new questions. Why did this number change? Why is revenue higher than last month? What caused this spike?

“These are exactly the questions a static graph, no matter how interactive, struggles to answer,” he explains. That’s what he defines as the data-business gap.

Stan saw this play out repeatedly in dashboard projects. “You’re always balancing,” he says. “From the data, you can derive all kinds of insights and KPIs. But if you include too much, users don’t know what they’re looking at or where to click. If you simplify too much, they come back asking why they can’t find this or that. Or they might have additional questions in any case.”

Traditionally, bridging this data-business gap meant manual work: analysts digging through SQL queries, pulling ad-hoc reports, and answering questions on demand.

That’s what pushed Stan to experiment with agentic AI. “I thought that if AI can write SQL, it can perhaps handle data analysis too.”

About two years ago, he and a colleague ran a two-day “pressure cooker” prototype – just a small local database and a language model generating queries. The system surfaced answers faster than writing SQL by hand, and also enabled users with no technical knowledge to access the data themselves.

“That was an eye-opener for me,” he says, “and I see people increasingly interact with data through natural language only.”


--- Using agentic AI in practice ---

One example Stan shares comes from a project with a large railway infrastructure organization in the Netherlands, working on the electricity network around train operations.

The project started with consolidating scattered data and building dashboards, before moving to predictive models that estimate risk ranges for extreme load situations.

The platform was strong, with clean data, solid models, and valuable predictions, Stan says, but something was missing: combining all the insights and providing them to the user in a way that they can understand and actively interact with.

“ Why do I get this electricity peak load? What was it for the last year on this point? What will change in the future? Why is the load lower now? These are all diverse questions that you can’t put in one overview by default, but with agentic AI it’s possible to answer them and get closer to the user,” Stan says.


--- Building and integrating agents into a data architecture ---

When it comes to building AI agents, Stan and his team have experimented with different architectures, the main contenders being LangChain and PydanticAI, a Python package that includes agent functionality.

“In practice, LangChain felt a bit bloated for what we needed,” Stan explains. Many of its features also require sending data through a LangChain server. “We’re not working with conversational data – we’re working with raw tables – and we didn’t want to push that outside our environment.”

PydanticAI turned out to be a better fit. It’s more “bare-bones,” but structured and clean, and it gave them enough control to build a production-ready system without unnecessary complexity. For Stan, that made it a safer choice – both for data protection and for keeping the codebase simple and easy to maintain.

As for integrating agentic AI into an existing data architecture, Stan’s starting point is simple: where is the data stored? “If all your data is stored in one database, you only need to create one tool to contact it and query it for you. And then you need to connect this tool to the agent,” he says.

“But if the data is scattered, which is more often the case, you have to think: do I need to create a tool for every system or do I need to manually combine it first and then feed it to the agent?”

He describes two possible routes. The first is to create a tool to connect to every system, which might eventually confuse the agent. The second one is to bring similar data sources together in one place and let the agent query that single system.

In his projects, he usually chooses the second option. That choice doesn’t remove the hard work upfront: data cleaning and modeling. “Agentic AI isn’t a solution for data cleaning,” Stan says. “That part is still there, just like in every other AI project.”


--- Safeguards and trust ---

Stan is very clear about one thing: you can’t guarantee that an AI agent will always understand the context perfectly. “That was a big mindset shift for me,” he says. “With a model or a dashboard, you can measure accuracy and double-check every number. With an agent, you can’t get to 100% correctness on every question – and if that’s the requirement, you shouldn’t use one.”

What matters is the trade-off. “In many cases, people either have no answer at all, or they spend hours digging through data and files,” he says, arguing that if an agent can give a good answer in a minute with a high chance of being right, that’s already a big step forward – as long as users can tell whether they should trust it. That’s why transparency is central: Stan’s team always shows the queries the agent runs and the raw data it returns, so users can see exactly what the answer is based on.

“The interpretation of the data that generates the final answer could contain errors, but the models are typically good enough now.”

Changing the system prompt can change the agent’s behaviour, so the team maintains example questions and expected answers and runs them as end-to-end tests. They use an “LLM as a judge” – a second model that checks whether the agent’s output still matches what it should do –  and are currently experimenting with token uncertainty (log probabilities) to estimate model confidence. If uncertainty is too high, the agent simply refuses to answer, returning: “Sorry, I can’t answer this question.”


--- Biggest learnings ---

For Stan, the biggest learning was a mindset shift in how he thinks about value. “With a predictive model, you can measure accuracy and often translate that into clear business impact,” he says. “With an agent, it’s different. You have much less control over the value, and you can’t easily verify it yourself – the value really depends on how people use it.”

His advice to other data scientists and engineers is to just start. “When I studied five years ago, I’d never heard of agents,” he says. “I just started experimenting when I saw the potential.” The field changes so quickly that being 'late' doesn’t really exist. “Within a year, most of what I know will probably be outdated. If you start now, you can catch up fast.”

Looking ahead, Stan thinks two things need to improve. First, the models. Most well-performing models today are still very general: they’re very good at everything, but not great at specific things like querying data or understanding specific domains. If the model itself understood the data and the domain better, the agent would need far less prompt engineering and extra context, and answers would be right much more often.

Secondly, hardware. “If GPUs get more energy-efficient and faster,” he says, “we can run these big models locally.” That would make things safer, less expensive, and easier to use in practice – especially if agents really become the main way people work with data.

--- Introduction ---

Financial health plays an important role in how individuals and households experience stability in their daily lives and confidence about the future. It affects not only people’s ability to manage their finances or cope with setbacks, but also their emotional wellbeing, stress levels, participation in society, and capacity to make long-term choices. Against this backdrop, we joined forces with ING – one of our founding partners – to address financial health as a shared societal challenge. >>> Evaluation Dataset <<<

Ideally, the evaluation dataset would consist of real conversations from real users. Unfortunately, this wasn’t possible for two reasons: protecting user privacy, and the fact that there isn't yet a deployed financial health chatbot from which we could get these conversations.

To solve this, we generated synthetic conversations. Writing these manually proved time-consuming and did not scale well, as it is difficult to realistically simulate a broad range of users and situations. Instead, we used an LLM that “simulates” a user.

We compiled a list of possible user personas (different ages, different financial and digital literacy, different occupations) and scenarios in which a user would contact a financial health chatbot (for example, a life event like job loss). These personas and scenarios were fed to the user-simulator LLM, which then generates questions for our chatbot.

Although this approach enables scalable data generation, simulated users are not perfectly realistic. For example, the LLM simulator tends to write with perfect grammar, spelling and punctuation, whereas a real user would be more inconsistent. Sometimes, the simulator also repeats itself or asks meaningless questions.

Therefore, the simulated questions have to be checked and – if necessary – corrected. To do this in an efficient way that doesn’t require coding skills, we built a small helper app called the Conversation Editor. This tool allows reviewers to adjust both the simulated user questions and the chatbot responses, correcting errors and improving realism.

With this tooling in place – the personas, scenarios, simulator, and editor – we were able to create many diverse conversations to evaluate the chatbot on. During the challenge, the participants could generate additional conversations to test new features. These new conversations were also used to make sure that certain bugs or unexpected behavior in the chatbot answers would be caught. They were then added to the evaluation dataset.

At the same time, customer journey experts and a dedicated conversation specialist were continuously testing the chatbot and adding new conversations to the dataset. The focus of those conversations was to evaluate the tone and style of the answers, rather than factuality and correctness.


>>> Evaluation Criteria <<<

In traditional machine learning and deep learning, evaluations are relatively straightforward: the model output is compared to an expected output, and the result is marked as a pass or fail. However, with generative AI this process is harder.

A chatbot can produce a correct and useful answer even when its wording differs from the expected response, and many evaluation questions require subjective judgement. For example, did the chatbot provide financial advice, or did it merely inform the user about available options?

In an ideal setting, such evaluations would employ human expert graders that judge the chatbot responses. In practice, however, it’s impractical to ask a human to assess dozens or hundreds of questions and answers every time you make a small change.

Therefore, we opted for an automated solution. One approach was using LLMs that are instructed to automatically judge the output of the chatbot – essentially acting as a proxy for human judges.

Initially, the set of judges were set up based on the project requirements. Over the course of the challenge, additional judges were introduced based on issues observed during development. For example, at some point the chatbot forgot previous messages in a conversation and greeted the user again midway through the conversation. To make sure that this did not happen again, the team added the “coherence judge.”

Here are some examples of the LLM-based judges we used:

Follow-up questions : Does the chatbot ask exactly one follow-up question, either to gather more information or to keep the conversation flowing? Is the follow-up question relevant? This ensures that the chatbot doesn’t give premature solutions or delay providing a clear answer.

Non-Advice : The LLM judges if the answer stays clear from giving specific financial advice but instead informs and educates the user about their options. This is critical for risk mitigation and legal compliance.

There are limitations with LLM judges, however. They suffer from the same shortcoming as the chatbot itself: the variability in their responses and bias. They can occasionally produce outright wrong evaluations or misunderstand the user intent, or the intent of the expected response. Additionally, they can be relatively slow (and expensive) to run: having 10 judges, requires 10 requests to an LLM judge to evaluate a single chatbot answer. This directly impacts the speed of iteration and development feedback, which can be crucial in a two-week hackathon.

Therefore, the team came up with non-LLM based metrics that are fast to run, reproducible, and deterministic. For example:

URL checker: Checks that the links in a reply do actually exist in the retrieval database.

Readability: Uses the Flesch Reading Ease score to determine how readable a chatbot reply is. We aim for a score of 70, which corresponds to 7th grade reading level.

Word count: Simply counting the words in the chatbot reply to see if the bot keeps it concise.

In practice, LLM and non-LLM based metrics were used together to get a holistic picture of the evaluation.

At this point, it is nevertheless worth mentioning that evaluations alone are not sufficient to create a reliable chatbot system. LLMs can generate plausible answers even when their input is corrupted due to a bug in the code, as in one instance when the chatbot started greeting users again mid-conversation. Such issues can remain silent unless they are explicitly tested for, making classical software best practices, such as unit testing and monitoring, more important than ever.

Throughout the challenge, the evaluation team monitored the work of the other teams to ensure that the whole group made progress. New features often introduced new issues, which in turn led to the addition of new test cases and judges to monitor those problems and implement fixes.

As a result, the actual evaluation scores kept fluctuating significantly during the challenge. This was because the evaluation framework itself was not fixed but constantly changed together with the capabilities of the chatbot, becoming progressively stricter as more test cases and judges were added.

Consequently, although we made a lot of qualitative improvements to the chatbot, the evaluation scores over the two weeks do not directly reflect that. Nevertheless, the work of the evaluation team was invaluable as it provided guidance and feedback to the other teams.

The “Understanding and Informing” team was key in customizing the chatbot. The first step to tailoring a generic chatbot to a specific context is to write a system prompt that instructs the chatbot about its role, its tasks, boundaries, and capabilities.

The core objective of this work package was to:

Understand: Establish a deep understanding of the user's financial context, especially when they are in severe financial distress. Obtain this information purely through empathetic conversation.

Personalize: Adapt the chatbot's tone and communication style (formal vs. informal, empathetic vs. factual) to the user’s style of communication while preserving their anonymity.

In collaboration with ING’s conversation specialist and the evaluation team, this team formulated explicit prompts to instruct the bot to adapt its answer tone based on the detected user persona. The team adopted an investigation strategy to ask only one focused question at a time to avoid overwhelming the user and to gather the minimum necessary information for a hand-off or a tailored response (the "One Question" rule).

Prompting – even begging – the chatbot LLM to only ask one question at a time didn’t convince the chatbot to do so. Very often it would ask multiple questions. To address this, the team found a simple trick: instead of letting the chatbot generate free text, they enforced a structured output that allowed only the following three components:

Reasoning: The LLM generates a step-by-step approach to analyze the situation and give an answer. This is not shown to the user.

Reasoning: The LLM generates a step-by-step approach to analyze the situation and give an answer. This is not shown to the user.

Response: The main answer containing the requested information.

Follow-up question: The follow-up question to ask.

By enforcing this structure, the chatbot consistently asked only one follow-up question. Additionally, the response quality also improved because the LLM had room to create a plan and reflect on its instructions in the reasoning stage, before creating a response. This technique is known as “chain-of-thought”. Current LLMs perform this reasoning (also called “thinking”) automatically and don’t have to be prompted to do so. However, that process can not be controlled, which is why we made it explicit in the chatbot output structure.


>>> Multi-agent Architecture <<<

Writing a single prompt for all the different scenarios and edge cases can lead to an overly large system prompt, which may contain conflicting instructions that can confuse the chabot. Therefore, the team set up a multi-agent architecture in collaboration with the “Tool Use” team. In this architecture, each user question is forwarded to a chatbot “within” the chatbot (a so-called “subagent”). Each subagent is responsible for a specific topic, such as mortgages, investments, or credit cards. These subagents have their own prompts and also their own specific tools (see Tool Use ).

In addition to the topic-specific subagents, the team added a “customer intake” subagent, responsible for gathering information about the user’s situation. This agent is the default agent being activated if the topic or the intent of the question is not yet clear.

But how do we decide which subagent to invoke for a certain user question? We can use another agent! For every user question, the conversation is passed to a faster language model that has only one task: return the name of the subagent that should process this question. We call this the “routing agent.”

One special type of agent in our architecture is always active: the RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) agent. This agent generates search queries  for the retrieval engine to fetch websites that are relevant to the user question. Every chatbot answer should be grounded in a website, which is why the RAG agent is constantly active – while for simple inputs like “Hello!,” it  can also decide not to perform a search.

Introducing multiple subagents created a new challenge. Each subagent required instructions for answer style, which led to duplicated prompt content and, over time, inconsistent responses across subagents. To resolve this, the team added a “final answer agent.” This agent synthesizes the information collected by the subagents as well as website snippets from the RAG agent. It then formulates a concise, understandable response that is sent back to the user. The prompt for the final answer agent includes instructions for the tone, style, and structure of the response.


--- Work package 3: Tool use & Retrieval ---

Tools are pieces of software that the chatbot can use to perform a certain task, such as calculations, information retrieval, or even scheduling appointments. For each user input, the chatbot can decide whether it can answer directly or whether a tool is required. If a tool is needed, the chatbot generates the input for that tool. The tool is then executed and the results are fed back to the chatbot. Based on these results, the chatbot can either generate a response for the user or decide to invoke another tool, repeating this process as necessary.

Each of the six agents has access to a specific tool set which it needs to perform its duties. In total, the team implemented 22 tools during the challenge.

For a financial application for example, calculators are an important tool as language models are not reliable with math. Current LLM models can do simple calculations like additions and multiplications but struggle with exponentials like compound interest calculations for investment returns.


>>> Retrieval <<<

Retrieval refers to the process of collecting approved websites that are relevant to the user question and can provide this information to the chatbot, so it can generate a response with verifiable sources

Subsequently, the websites are fed to the chatbot so that it can generate a response with sources that the user can check.

Initially, retrieval was implemented as a tool that individual agents could invoke when needed. However, because every informational response needed to be grounded in trusted sources, we didn’t want to rely on the chatbot or the subagents to decide whether they would use the tool. This is why we added a dedicated retrieval subagent (the “RAG agent” mentioned above) which is invoked for every user interaction and generates a retrieval query for the retrieval engine.

For the retrieval engine we had several options at the start of the challenge:

A self-hosted vector database that is integrated with the chatbot application

A self-hosted vector database that is integrated with the chatbot application

A vector database hosted by Google Cloud (Vertex RAG)

A vector database hosted by Google Cloud (Vertex RAG)

A hybrid text search and vector search database, also hosted on Google Cloud (Vertex AI Search)

To determine which retrieval engine performs, the team created an evaluation pipeline that used synthetic question-answer pairs, generated from the retrieval corpus with the following methodology:

Source Document Sampling: We selected a diverse and representative set of websites from the knowledge base. For each selection, we identified a few paragraphs or "chunks of text" that are thematically or contextually related. This ensures that the generated questions require a multi-website or multi-chunk synthesis to answer.

Question and Answer Generation: We employed an LLM to analyze the selected, related chunks of text. The LLM's task was to:

Generate a Question: Create a complex, natural language question that requires synthesizing information from the selected chunks to form a complete answer. These questions should simulate genuine user queries.

Mark the Source: The original chunks of text that contain the necessary information to answer the generated question must be explicitly marked and associated with that question-answer pair. These marked chunks serve as the "ground truth" source for validation during the retrieval phase.

Scaling the Process: This process must be repeated a substantial number of    times to create a diverse evaluation dataset. The goal is to cover various document topics, question types (e.g., factual, comparative, descriptive), and levels of difficulty.

Retrieval Metrics:

Guardrails, defined as rigid safety mechanisms, are necessary to contain the chatbot's unpredictable generative capacity and ensure it remains helpful without becoming illegal, unethical, or starting to hallucinate.

=== Why (and how) we need to measure data diversity in language models ===

Recent advances in language models are typically credited to improvements of architecture and ever-larger datasets. But a parallel conversation has been building – in research labs and across industry – around a more fundamental question: not just how much data these models are trained on, but what that data actually contains.

Within this context, Dong Nguyen – Associate Professor of Computer Science at Utrecht University – points out that data diversity is frequently cited as an important factor, and as one of the main reasons why models may show bias or fail on unusual cases during interaction.

“But when you take a step back,’ Nguyen says, “it’s very unclear what we mean with data diversity.”

The term, she notes, can refer to many different things, and without the necessary theoretical and practical insights, it’s difficult to make substantial claims about data diversity’s role. “How much does diversity matter? Which types of diversity are relevant? At what stages of training does diversity matter the most, and does its importance vary across tasks?”

These open questions are at the core of DataDivers , the research project Nguyen is currently leading. Funding by the EU and part of the NLP and Society Lab of Utrecht University, the project aims to examine how data diversity can be defined and measured in datasets, how it relates to the behaviour of models, and how it might be taken into account to develop fairer and more robust models.

“We’re focusing on LLMs at the moment, but a lot of the things that we do are quite general,” Nguyen notes, adding that the methods are intended to apply more broadly across Natural Language Processing (NLP) beyond large language models alone.


--- Measuring data diversity ---

According to Nguyen, the team’s main goal is to develop different methods that make it possible to measure data diversity more systematically. At the moment, their work focuses on two broad axes. The first is what she describes as stylistic diversity.

“Within any language,” she explains, “people don’t always write or speak in the same way. Expression varies depending on background, but also on the situation: writing a job application, for instance, is very different from speaking with a friend. The question then becomes how this kind of variation is represented in datasets, and whether it can be measured in a meaningful way.”

The second axis is semantic diversity, which relates more directly to the meaning of text. This includes variation in topics and subject matter, and how such differences are distributed across a dataset. Although the project will later look at other types of diversity, these two were chosen first because they’re expected to be relevant across many different applications.

From a technical perspective, Nguyen explains that the approaches to measuring stylistic and semantic diversity are quite similar. The core idea in both cases is to map texts into a vector space. For semantic diversity, texts that are similar in meaning end up close to each other in that space. For stylistic diversity, the goal is comparable: texts written in similar styles are placed near each other, while texts written in very different styles are positioned further apart.

“In this way, a dataset can be represented as a large cloud of points in a space,” Nguyen says. From there, it’s possible to apply different metrics to quantify diversity.

“We’re currently trying to categorize the different ways of measuring diversity and look at the pros and cons of each metric. At the same time, we’re setting up more systematic experiments to investigate which metrics work better than others and to formalize them further.”


--- Potential impact on models ---

Once clearer metrics for data diversity are in place, they can be used to investigate more systematically the impact of data composition on model behavior, Nguyen explains.

“If we vary the diversity in the dataset along different  axes,” she says, “it becomes possible to study whether this influences how well the models perform on certain tasks or the kind of linguistic variation present in their outputs.”

Building on this, Nguyen ties data diversity more broadly to the learning process of language models. She compares it to human learning: when acquiring a new language or skill, it would be limiting to rely on a narrow set of examples, instead of exposure to a broad and varied range of material.

Similarly, she expects diversity in training data to improve the learning process and points to early – though still preliminary – evidence that better and more diverse datasets may allow models to learn more effectively with less data.

From this perspective, a related goal of the project is to support the creation of better datasets from the outset. “What often happens nowadays is that, once a model's released, we discover all kinds of problems.”

“But,” she argues, “it all starts with the data. If we can understand the data and its influence on model behavior better, we could intervene earlier, before the training and deployment phases.”

At present, however, many decisions about how datasets are constructed and adjusted are still guided by heuristics and remain an “ad hoc” practice. This is partly due to constraints in data availability, but also because, as Nguyen notes, it is difficult to investigate these questions systematically without concrete frameworks or tools to measure diversity in the first place.


--- Challenges ---

Nguyen points to several challenges when attempting to measure data diversity in practice. A first difficulty is conceptual: what actually constitutes a good diversity metric is not always straightforward. “There isn’t just one definition, is there? It really depends on the specific application.”

A second challenge concerns scalability. “Many metrics that are theoretically appealing don’t scale well to the large datasets used for LLMs,” she adds, which might call for certain trade-offs between theoretical elegance and practical usability.

Finally, there is the issue of missing information. Many important axes of diversity, such as dialect or demographic representation, are simply not labeled in most large datasets, which are often collected from the web without rich metadata. “While it may be possible to infer some of this information automatically, the absence of reliable annotations makes it harder to develop fine-grained measures of diversity,” Nguyen says. “This is why our project focuses primarily on metrics that don’t rely on metadata."

But these challenges, she argues, aren’t insurmountable. The team is currently at the first stage of the project, which will result in the release of a software library, containing tools and methods that can be used to measure data diversity. The focus will then shift to investigating the impact of data diversity on model behaviour.

“If we can understand that link better, we can create fairer and more robust models that can handle different types of output more effectively,” Nguyen says.

--- AI lessons from the boardroom ---


=== Inside the Police’s AI approach to environmental crime ===


>>> Police innovator Amir Niknam on environmental crime, complexity and the limits of technology <<<

By Rutger Huizenga

Executives are increasingly confronted with AI. Not as a gadget, but as a governance question: what should you use it for, who remains responsible, and what should you not use it for? In the new interview series "AI Lessons from the Boardroom," we speak with people who have to make those decisions in practice. Today: Amir Niknam, senior innovation advisor at the Dutch National Police, about how AI helps him combat environmental crime.

Niknam is not a typical technology executive. He studied psychology, worked in IT for many years and started at the police as a detective. Since last year he has been part of the High Impact Environmental Crimes team, which deals with the most serious and complex environmental cases in the Netherlands and internationally. These range from illegal waste dumping on industrial sites to cases involving international supply chains, permits, chemical substances and major financial interests.

Environmental crimes are close to Niknam’s heart. He even supported the Extinction Rebellion demonstrations against fossil fuel subsidies that blocked the A12 motorway, of course without breaking any rules or hindering colleagues on duty. Environmental safety, he says, is not activism but an urgent security issue.

“The damage is less visible than street violence, but the impact on health and living conditions is enormous. Some Dutch cities demonstrably face years shorter life expectancy because of air pollution. And we need to stop that.”


--- Playing legal Champions League ---

Environmental cases are notoriously difficult. A single investigation often touches simultaneously on criminal law, administrative law, permits and European regulations. Money and goods flow through producers, transporters and traders, often across borders.

“Anyone who wants to intervene here has to play in the legal Champions League”, says Niknam. “Take the wrong approach and you’re stuck in endless procedures. And time tends to favor companies with deep pockets and strong legal teams. Complexity can become a shield.”

The bottleneck often appears at the start of a case. There may be a signal or report, but what is the most promising legal route? That first analysis currently takes detectives weeks or even months, while the damage continues.


--- "Al Capone" as a principle ---

This is exactly where Niknam and KickstartAI are focusing with an AI tool currently under development. The project, winner of the National AI Challenge 2025, has internally been nicknamed Al Capone.

The reference is telling: the gangster was not convicted of murder, but of tax evasion.

“You see that principle in environmental cases as well”, Niknam explains. “The most serious violation is not always the easiest to prove. Sometimes it’s more effective to revoke a permit or shut down a company than to wait for a drawn-out criminal trial.”

The AI tool only supports the initial analysis phase. An intelligence officer enters context, a report, an anomaly, a suspicion. The system searches accessible legislation, case law and similar cases, but it does not issue judgments.

“You want to see more quickly where the legal opening might be. After that, it remains human work.”

Niknam stresses that he is not responsible for the overall AI policy of the police. “I’m speaking here in a personal capacity.” Yet the questions he encounters, about responsibility, boundaries and legitimacy, are broader ones. Within the police, AI is not introduced lightly, he says.

“We operate in a constitutional state. That requires explainability, proportionality and human judgment.”

It also means that not everything that is technically possible is automatically desirable.

“The police are vigilant and serve the values of the democratic rule of law. That is our mission, and it must always come first.”


--- The broader lesson ---

This is also the lesson he wants to share with other organizations. “Never let AI become a strategy in itself. It should always be a means.”

In his own work, the starting point was very concrete: the slow start of complex environmental investigations. Not reforming the entire police organization, but addressing one clear bottleneck.

According to him, that’s where things often go wrong. Organizations start with the technology instead of the friction.

“The danger is that new technology ends up reinforcing the very problem you’re trying to solve”, he says.

He also sees how many organizations are still structured linearly, according to the model of the industrial revolution: step one, then step two, then step three. That worked in factories, but in knowledge work it leads to waiting times, handovers and layers of control.

“You end up with gatekeepers everywhere. Another check, another form. And the system gradually clogs up.”

The temptation is to replace those gatekeepers with AI. “But that’s like buying a better mop while the tap is still running.”


--- Building blocks instead of chains ---

According to Niknam, the real intervention lies in the design of the process itself. Why must work constantly be handed over? Why not organize it more asynchronously? He advocates working with modular building blocks, like a house where you can renovate one room without demolishing the entire building.

In such a system, AI is a tool, not a load-bearing wall. “AI must be replaceable. Switchable off when the context changes. If you can’t disconnect it without everything collapsing, you have a problem.

Because if the structure itself is flawed, AI won’t fix it. “It will only make things go wrong faster.”

“Don’t wait because the protocol says so”. Niknam concludes. “Act as soon as you can. And that’s exactly where AI can be a wonderful tool.”

“AI can add value in thousands of places,” he says matter-of-factly. “But that is exactly the danger. If you start everywhere, you often end up nowhere.”

It is a lesson he learned in practice at the bank, where he started eight years ago as a management trainee and rose to global leadership of the operations connected to risk management. Technological choices become reality faster when the entire organization has the right focus.

“Everyone sees opportunities in AI, and those opportunities are real. But in a large organization, the temptation is strong to experiment in many places at once. Before long, you get a proliferation of pilots, proofs of concept, and isolated ideas that never truly land in the core process. The art is to say: this is where we will invest, and this is where we will not. That sounds simple, but it is not. Because it also means saying no to good ideas, to initiatives that may seem smarter or more efficient in themselves.”


--- Critical thinking ---

In the operations domain of Risk, for which he is responsible, Dusée clearly sees the difference AI can make. Much of the work involves assessing huge volumes of information: plans, validations, controls, and documentation. A large part of that is still manual and time-consuming. According to Dusée, AI can add a great deal of value here, for example by extracting, organizing, and analyzing information more quickly. But for him, the biggest change is not about speed. It is about what AI asks of people.

“AI forces employees to work more critically,” says Dusée. “You have to learn not only how to use it, but also how to question it. Where are the limits? When do you trust the output, and when do you not? What thresholds do you apply? That requires expertise, not blind trust.”

He adds: “The pace of change is high. Two years ago, we were talking about chatbots; now we are already talking about agentic AI and conversational AI. That is why you should not only train employees on today’s tools, but above all teach them how to keep adapting, spot opportunities, and help steer AI adoption.”

That is why ING, he says, is deliberately investing in training, with programs for both employees and leaders. Not because everyone needs to become a technical specialist, but because AI does not work if it remains a toy for a small group of experts.

“Often, someone spots an opportunity within their own scope, for example when creating a report. That is a good start, but for leaders the real work only begins after that: can you apply such a solution more broadly? Not for one report, but for all reports, or perhaps even for the entire process? That requires choices. You cannot do everything at once. Sometimes you have to say very explicitly: these are the six use cases we will focus on this year, and we will leave those other twenty aside for now.”

Continuing to challenge and question yourself is also important when it comes to changing processes through AI, he emphasizes.

“The first reflex is often: how can I add AI while keeping everything else the same? But the more interesting question is: should this process actually be fundamentally redesigned?”

At ING, he sees this, for example, in the manual processes used to collect data and make it available for models. The question is not only which step can be made faster, but whether the entire chain should be redesigned.

“Only then does AI become a strategic question,” he says. “Not because executives are operating the technology themselves, but because they determine what gets priority, how it is embedded and who is responsible for it.”

That, according to Dusée, is where things often go wrong in practice. Not with the technology, but with the organization.

“Many pilots fail because organizations think about day two too late. Teams build something in a safe environment and it works. But only then does the real question arise: where does this land in the organization? In which process? Which team becomes the owner? How do you maintain it? How does it connect to existing systems? If you only start asking those questions after the pilot, you get friction. Other teams have not been involved, the processes do not align, or there is simply no capacity to roll it out. Then something remains stuck in a kind of innovation limbo.”

To prevent this, ING has set up a clear structure for AI: a central analytics team that oversees the portfolio, while concrete ideas can still come from the shop floor.

“That central direction is essential to prevent teams from reinventing the wheel in their own small corner. The aim is to work in an ‘agnostic’ way: a solution that works for one type of reporting should be immediately scalable to all reporting within the bank.”


--- KickstartAI ---

The questions Dusée encounters within ING are by no means unique to banks, he says. That is precisely why he is active within KickstartAI, the partnership involving organizations including KLM, NS, ING and Ahold Delhaize, which aims to accelerate AI adoption in the Netherlands. The sectors may differ, but the underlying AI challenges are often much more similar.

“KLM’s market is completely different from ING’s,” he says. “But you encounter the same challenges. From governance and data to the question: how do you move from a proof of concept to a real product?” For organizations, he believes, the value is immediate: learning faster what works and what does not.

For Dusée, KickstartAI also has a broader meaning than knowledge exchange between large companies. He explicitly sees it as a way to create more societal impact. As an example, he mentions an AI tool around financial health that ING is currently exploring  together with KickstartAI, with the aim of eventually investigating how customers can be helped earlier and more accessibly when financial problems are at risk of emerging.

Dusée: “How do you reach people earlier, lower barriers and offer help in a way that matches what people need? I find that at least as relevant. If we approach AI only as a machine for working more efficiently, we are selling ourselves short.”

But for Dusée, the value of KickstartAI extends beyond the partners alone. He sees the network not only as a place where large organizations exchange knowledge, but also as a way to make expertise more broadly available.

“There are so many organizations in the Netherlands, both profit and non-profit, where AI can be incredibly valuable,” he says. “In education, research, and healthcare, for example. But not everyone has the financial resources or capacity to apply AI quickly. How valuable is it, then, if we can contribute through KickstartAI and make our knowledge available?”

That is why he hopes more organizations will join. Not because every partner has to do the same thing, but because a larger network, in his view, makes more possible: more exchange, more learning capacity and ultimately more societal impact.

Dusée: “Those who work together not only increase the chances of scaling within their own organization, but also of creating real societal impact.”


--- Five lessons from Leon Dusée for other organizations ---

1. Choose sharply, and consciously leave things aside

According to Dusée, the biggest mistake is not experimenting too little, but wanting to do too much at once. Leaders must dare to say: this is where we will focus, and not there for now. Without focus, AI remains fragmented.

2. Look beyond the individual use case to the entire process

Many ideas rightly emerge bottom-up, but remain too small. The leadership question, he says, is always: can you broaden an application from one report or task to the entire process? That is where real scale lies.

3. Think about day two from day one

Pilots often stall because ownership, maintenance and embedding in existing processes are considered too late. Do not optimize for the demo, but for practice.

4. Invest as much in people as in technology

AI raises the demands placed on employees. Subject-matter expertise, adaptability and the ability to challenge output become more important, not less. Dusée also explicitly mentions learning on the job and a different perspective on education.

5. See AI as societal infrastructure too

According to Dusée, AI should not only be about productivity. Especially in sectors such as education, research and healthcare, collaboration can help make applications more accessible to organizations that do not themselves have the resources to lead the way.

--- AI lessons from the boardroom ---


=== KLM CIO Aart Slagt on why an AI model only has value if users understand and trust it ===

AI is now on almost every agenda. Not as a gadget, but as a strategic question: what should you use it for, how do you scale it, and how do you prevent experiments from getting stuck in the pilot phase? In the interview series "AI lessons from the boardroom," we speak with executives and board members who have to make those choices in practice. In this edition: Aart Slagt, Executive Vice President Information Services & CIO at KLM Royal Dutch Airlines. “The time for experimentation alone is over. We are now focused on truly implementing AI at the core of our business.”

Aart Slagt has already had a full day by the time he sits down to talk about artificial intelligence. Meetings, coordination, decisions, operational questions: as CIO of KLM, he constantly moves between technology and practice. That also fits his profile. Slagt is not an executive of fashionable buzzwords, but someone who comes from operations and still looks at things through that lens. After ten years in operational roles and now five years as CIO, he knows that plans fall apart if they do not resonate with colleagues on the operational side.

“We, too, have gone through a phase of experimentation here.” It’s been a useful stage, says Slagt, “because it helped the business and IT understand each other better.” But as far as he is concerned, that phase is now over. “We can no longer let a thousand flowers bloom. We want to create focus: where does KLM want to deploy AI to the fullest, which processes does the company want to redesign from an AI-first perspective, and where do we deliberately choose not to apply it?”

Not every part of KLM lends itself to far-reaching automation, Slagt knows. Some things you simply want people to do. “Take cabin service on our aircraft. That cannot exist without human contact.” But in other areas, AI can make a real difference, and that is exactly where you want to accelerate it: in planning, optimization, administration, and support. A good example is the model KLM built together with KickstartAI to better predict how much catering is needed on board , based on the actual number of passengers who end up taking the flight. It sounds small, but the impact is significant. “On the one hand, you can serve passengers better with the catering that is really needed,” says Slagt. “On the other hand, you prevent a huge amount of food waste, because far less has to be thrown away. So it is a double win.”


--- Trust and explainability ---

Another example Slagt is proud of concerns a joint project on contingency fuel : the additional fuel an aircraft carries for emergencies and unexpected circumstances. It naturally requires a delicate balance. Safety always comes first, but carrying too much fuel is also unsustainable and costly. “If you need it, it has to be on board. But you also do not want to take too much, because it adds unnecessary weight.”

Together with KickstartAI, KLM built a model that takes a higher number of variables into account than the traditional approach. Think of load, passenger numbers, wind, weather systems, and conditions at the destination. It is a unique model, but “ultimately, the captain is responsible and decides how much fuel is taken on board. So it is important that you can explain how such a model works, which variables go into it, and why the advice comes out the way it does.”


--- Knowledge ---

And that, according to Slagt, is exactly where things often go wrong. “A model may look excellent on paper, but if people do not understand it, nothing changes on the work floor.” That is precisely why it is important for end users (in this case pilots as well as the pilots’ union,) to be involved early in the development of such an application. And it is equally important for executives to understand both the capabilities and the limitations of AI. Slagt says: “If you have to make decisions about investments, risks, and applications, you cannot follow this from a distance. You really have to immerse yourself in what AI can and cannot do.”

At KLM, that meant the leadership team did not get yet another presentation, but instead had to sit down behind a laptop themselves, in this case at Google’s office. Slagt: “The assignment was simple: build your own AI assistant for an advertising campaign. Or create an agent that scans aviation news every week and puts it in your inbox. Only when you do it yourself do you see what AI can  deliver.”

KLM noticed the same thing when generative AI started advancing rapidly and employees wanted to start working with it. That is why KLM built Talia: a secure environment in which employees can safely work with models such as Gemini, Mistral, and ChatGPT. “It is important to keep building the bridge between the theory behind AI and its practical application. The colleagues that will work with it need to understand it, embrace it, and gain trust in it.”


--- The Dutch debate ---

But according to Slagt, the learning doesn't stop there. Anyone who really wants to make serious progress with AI also has to look outside their company.For that reason, KLM is active in KickstartAI, an initiative it co-founded with NS, Ahold, and ING. For Slagt, the value lies not only in the technology, but also in the way of collaborating: companies, sectors, and specialists coming together to solve real problems and put AI on the map in the Netherlands.

He calls KickstartAI, not without reason, “the coalition of the doing.” It is not a network of endless meetings and discussion groups, but a foundation that simply gets to work and delivers strong results.

And according to Slagt, that is exactly where the gain lies. “By joining forces with other companies, you bring in AI knowledge as well as  a fresh perspective on your own processes. It is precisely that combination of data and AI specialists with people who sometimes come from completely different sectors that produces the best solutions. I would like to see more companies in the Netherlands benefit from that by joining KickstartAI.”

It is a pragmatic and open perspective that is still far from self-evident in the Netherlands, Slagt observes. “ We sometimes focus too much on the harm AI could cause,” he says. “And then we surround the technology with fences, rules, and restrictions.”

A shame, Slagt believes. Because AI can make companies more productive, organize processes more intelligently, and strengthen the Netherlands as a knowledge economy. At the same time, international developments are not standing still. Other countries are investing heavily in AI and rapidly building up knowledge and applications. “If we do not do it, someone else will,” he concludes. “Then we will be left behind. It would be a great shame if we let that happen.”


--- Three lessons from Aart Slagt for other organizations ---

1. Stop open-ended experimentation in time Pilots and proof-of-concepts are necessary to explore possibilities and bring business and IT closer together. But at a certain point, an organization has to make choices. Where do you really want to deploy AI, what do you want to scale up, and where do you deliberately choose not to apply the technology?

2. Do not build a model for the technology, build it for the user An application only creates value if the people who have to work with it understand it and trust it. Especially in regulated environments, AI must be explainable, align with professional responsibility, and be developed together with end users. That is why, for example, KLM involved pilots at a very early stage in the development process of its contingency fuel application.

3. Make AI a board-level issue, not a specialist silo Invest in the knowledge level of the company’s leadership. Anyone who wants to steer investments, risks, and implementation must personally understand what the technology can and cannot do. That requires not only vision, but also hands-on experience.
